Theseus</b>, also known as <b>Theseus' paradox</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Thought_experiment" title="Thought experiment">thought experiment</a> that raises the question of whether an object which has had all of its components replaced remains fundamentally <a href="/wiki/Identity_(philosophy)" title="Identity (philosophy)">the same object</a>. The paradox is most notably recorded by <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Parallel_Lives" title="Parallel Lives">Life of Theseus</a></i> from the late first century. Plutarch asked whether a ship which was restored by replacing each and every one of its wooden parts remained the same ship.</p> <p>The paradox had been discussed by more ancient philosophers such as <a href="/wiki/Heraclitus" title="Heraclitus">Heraclitus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> prior to Plutarch's writings; and more recently by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes" title="Thomas Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a>. Several <a href="#Variations_of_the_paradox">variants</a> are known, notably "grandfather's axe". This thought experiment is "a model for the philosophers"; some say, "it remained the same," some saying, "it did not remain the same".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a class="toctext">References</span></a></li> </ul> </div> <p></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations_of_the_paradox">Variations of the paradox</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ship_of_Theseus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Variations of the paradox">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_philosophy">Ancient philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ship_of_Theseus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>This particular version of the paradox was first introduced in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">legend</a> as reported by the historian, biographer, and essayist <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>,</p> <blockquote class="templatequote"> <p>"The ship wherein <a href="/wiki/Theseus" title="Theseus">Theseus</a> and the youth of <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> returned from <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> had thirty oars, and was preserved by the Athenians down even to the time of <a href="/wiki/Demetrius_Phalereus" title="Demetrius Phalereus" class="mw-redirect">Demetrius Phalereus</a>, for they took away the old planks as they decayed, putting in new and stronger timber in their places, in so much that this ship became a standing example among the philosophers, for the logical question of things that grow; one side holding that the ship remained the same, and the other contending that it was not the same."</p> <div class="templatequotecite">—Plutarch,&#160;<cite><i>Theseus</i><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></cite></div> </blockquote> <p><span id="hobbes_planks"></span> Plutarch thus questions whether the ship would remain the same if it were entirely replaced, piece by piece. Centuries later, the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes" title="Thomas Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</a> introduced a further puzzle, wondering what would happen if the original planks were gathered up after they were replaced, and used to build a second ship.<sup id="cite_ref-hobbes_chis_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hobbes_chis-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Hobbes asked which ship, if either, would be considered the original Ship of Theseus.</p> <p>Another early variation involves a scenario in which Socrates and Plato exchange the parts of their carriages one by one until, finally, Socrates's carriage is made up of all the parts of Plato's original carriage and vice versa. The question is presented if or when they exchanged their carriages.</p> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enlightenment_era">Enlightenment era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ship_of_Theseus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Enlightenment era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>John Locke proposed a scenario regarding a favorite <a href="/wiki/Sock" title="Sock">sock</a> that develops a hole. He pondered whether the sock would still be the same after a patch was applied to the hole, and if it would be the same sock, would it still be the same sock after a second patch was applied, and a third, etc., until all of the material of the original sock has been replaced with patches.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>George Washington's axe (sometimes "my grandfather's axe") is the subject of an apocryphal story of unknown origin in which the famous artifact is "still George Washington's axe" despite having had both its head and handle replaced.</p> <blockquote class="templatequote"> <p><i>...as in the case of the owner of George Washington's axe which has three times had its handle replaced and twice had its head replaced!</i></p> <div class="templatequotecite">—Ray Broadus Browne,&#160;<cite><i>Objects of Special Devotion: Fetishism in Popular Culture</i>, p. 134<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></cite></div> </blockquote> <p>This has also been recited as "<a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abe Lincoln's</a> axe";<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Lincoln was well known for his ability with an axe, and axes associated with his life are held in various museums.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>The French equivalent is the story of Jeannot's knife, where the eponymous knife has had its blade changed fifteen times and its handle fifteen times, but is still the same knife.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> In some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" (October 2013)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> Spanish-speaking countries, Jeannot's knife is present as a proverb, though referred to simply as "the family knife". The principle, however, remains the same.</p> <p>The 1872 story "<a href="/wiki/Dr._Ox%27s_Experiment" title="Dr. Ox's Experiment">Dr. Ox's Experiment</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a> has a reference to Jeannot's knife apropos the Van Tricasse's family. In this family, since 1340, each time one of the spouses died, the other remarried with someone younger, who took the family name. Thus, the family can be said to have been a single marriage lasting through centuries, rather than a series of generations. A similar concept, but involving more than two persons at any given time, is described in some detail in <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">Robert Heinlein's</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Moon_Is_a_Harsh_Mistress" title="The Moon Is a Harsh Mistress">The Moon Is a Harsh Mistress</a></i> as a <a href="/wiki/Line_marriage" title="Line marriage" class="mw-redirect">line marriage</a>.</p> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_day">Modern day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ship_of_Theseus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/ArtReview" title="ArtReview">ArtReview</a></i>, Sam Jacob noted that <a href="/wiki/Sugababes" title="Sugababes">Sugababes</a>, a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> band,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> "were formed in 1998 [..] but one by one they left, till by September 2009 none of the founders remained in the band; each had been replaced by another member, just like the planks of Theseus’s boat."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The three original members reunited in 2011 under the name <a href="/wiki/Mutya_Keisha_Siobhan" title="Mutya Keisha Siobhan">Mutya Keisha Siobhan</a>, with the "original" Sugababes still in existence.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>In the popular BBC sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Only_Fools_and_Horses" title="Only Fools and Horses">Only Fools and Horses</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Trigger_(Only_Fools_and_Horses)" title="Trigger (Only Fools and Horses)">Trigger</a> (a roadsweeper) declares he has won an award for keeping the same broom for 20 years — "17 new heads and 14 new handles".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> This has become known as the "Trigger's broom" paradox.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>The rate of development of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> aircraft such as the <a href="/wiki/Supermarine_Spitfire" title="Supermarine Spitfire">Supermarine Spitfire</a> meant that the design of an aircraft at the end of the war could be substantially different from the "same" aircraft six years earlier. For example, late-war Spitfires had a different engine,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> propellor,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> cockpit,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> wings,<sup id="cite_ref-Morgan_and_Shacklady_2000.2C_p._493._19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan_and_Shacklady_2000.2C_p._493.-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> fuselage,<sup id="cite_ref-Morgan_and_Shacklady_2000.2C_p._494._22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan_and_Shacklady_2000.2C_p._494.-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> tail assembly,<sup id="cite_ref-Quill_1983.2C_p._272._23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Quill_1983.2C_p._272.-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> fuel tanks,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> weaponry,<sup id="cite_ref-Flintham_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Flintham-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> and control surface design<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> to the original Spitfire Mk I, leaving very few major components unchanged. This was recognized when the subsequent variant of the Spitfire was given a new designation, the <a href="/wiki/Supermarine_Spiteful" title="Supermarine Spiteful">Spiteful</a>.<sup class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_resolutions">Proposed resolutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ship_of_Theseus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Proposed resolutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heraclitus">Heraclitus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ship_of_Theseus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Heraclitus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>The Greek philosopher <a href="/wiki/Heraclitus" title="Heraclitus">Heraclitus</a> attempted to solve the paradox by introducing the idea of a river where water replenishes it. <a href="/wiki/Arius_Didymus" title="Arius Didymus">Arius Didymus</a> quoted him as saying "upon those who step into the same rivers, different and again different waters flow".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Plutarch disputed Heraclitus' claim about stepping twice into the same river, citing that it cannot be done because "it scatters and again comes together, and approaches and recedes".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aristotle.27s_causes">Aristotle's causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ship_of_Theseus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aristotle&#039;s causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophical</a> system of Aristotle and his followers, <a href="/wiki/Four_causes" title="Four causes">four causes</a> or reasons describe a thing; these causes can be analyzed to get to a solution to the paradox. The <a href="/wiki/Formal_cause" title="Formal cause" class="mw-redirect">formal cause</a> or 'form' is the design of a thing, while the <a href="/wiki/Material_cause" title="Material cause" class="mw-redirect">material cause</a> is the matter of which the thing is made. The "what-it-is" of a thing, according to Aristotle, is its formal cause, so the ship of Theseus is the 'same' ship, because the formal cause, or design, does not change, even though the matter used to construct it may vary with time. In the same manner, for Heraclitus's paradox, a river has the same formal cause, although the material cause (the particular water in it) changes with time, and likewise for the person who steps in the river.</p> <p>Another of Aristotle's causes is the 'end' or <a href="/wiki/Final_cause" title="Final cause" class="mw-redirect">final cause</a>, which is the intended purpose of a thing. The ship of Theseus would have the same ends, those being, mythically, transporting Theseus, and politically, convincing the Athenians that Theseus was once a living person, though its material cause would change with time. The <a href="/wiki/Efficient_cause" title="Efficient cause" class="mw-redirect">efficient cause</a> is how and by whom a thing is made, for example, how artisans fabricate and assemble something; in the case of the ship of Theseus, the workers who built the ship in the first place could have used the same tools and techniques to replace the planks in the ship.</p> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions_of_.22the_same.22">Definitions of "the same"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ship_of_Theseus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Definitions of &quot;the same&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>One common argument found in the philosophical literature is that in the case of Heraclitus' river one is tripped up by two different definitions of "the same". In one sense, things can be "qualitatively identical", by sharing some properties. In another sense, they might be "numerically identical" by being "one". As an example, consider two different marbles that look identical. They would be qualitatively, but not numerically, identical. A marble can be numerically identical only to itself.</p> <p>Note that some languages differentiate between these two forms of identity. In <a href="/wiki/German_(language)" title="German (language)" class="mw-redirect">German</a>, for example, "<i>gleich</i>" ("equal") and "<i>selbst</i>" ("self-same") are the pertinent terms, respectively. At least in formal speech, the former refers to qualitative identity (e.g. <i>die gleiche Murmel</i>, "the same [qualitative] marble") and the latter to numerical identity (e.g. <i>die selbe Murmel</i>, "the same [numerical] marble"). Colloquially, the terms are sometimes used interchangeably, however.</p> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Four-dimensionalism">Four-dimensionalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ship_of_Theseus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Four-dimensionalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Perdurantism" title="Perdurantism">Perdurantism</a></div> <p><a href="/wiki/Ted_Sider" title="Ted Sider" class="mw-redirect">Ted Sider</a> and others have proposed that considering objects to extend across time as four-dimensional causal series of three-dimensional 'time slices' could solve the ship of Theseus problem because, in taking such an approach, each time-slice and all four dimensional objects remain numerically identical to themselves while allowing individual time-slices to differ from each other.
