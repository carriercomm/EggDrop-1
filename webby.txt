<h1><a href="http://www.thetruthaboutcars.com/2013/09/why-do-police-cars-use-red-blue-lights-theyre-visually-confusing/" title="Permalink for Why Do Police Cars Use Red &#038; Blue Lights? They&#8217;re Visually Confusing">Why Do Police Cars Use Red &#038; Blue Lights? They&#8217;re Visually Confusing</a></h1>      </div>        <div class="postbyline">        By <span itemprop="author"><a href="http://www.thetruthaboutcars.com/author/ronnie-schreiber/" title="Posts by Ronnie Schreiber" rel="author">Ronnie Schreiber</a></span> on September 30, 2013      </div><!-- end postbyline -->    <script type="text/javascript">reddit_newwindow='1'</script>   <ul class="socialbuttons top">    <li class="fb"><span class="fb_share"><fb:like href="http://www.thetruthaboutcars.com/2013/09/why-do-police-cars-use-red-blue-lights-theyre-visually-confusing/" send="false" layout="button_count" width="72" data-action="recommend"></fb:like> </span></li>    <li class="tweet"><a href="https://twitter.com/share" class="twitter-share-button" data-lang="en">Tweet</a></li>    <li class="gplus"><div class="g-plus" data-action="share" data-annotation="bubble"></div></li>    <li class="reddit"><script type="text/javascript" src="http://www.reddit.com/static/button/button1.js"></script></li>   </ul>         <div class="postbody">                   <!-- google_ad_section_start --><p style="text-align: center;"><span class="vvqbox vvqyoutube" style="width:450px;height:364px;"><span id="vvq-519881-youtube-1"><a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wt9COaHv57E"><img src="http://img.youtube.com/vi/wt9COaHv57E/0.jpg" alt="YouTube Preview Image" /></a></span></span></p> <p style="text-align: left;">Sorry for the tease but to get the full effect of this post you&#8217;re going to have to click on Read More. It&#8217;s not that we want the additional clicks, it&#8217;s just that I&#8217;m using a graphic to illustrate this post that is so eye-searing that the layout and graphic designer in me just couldn&#8217;t put it on the front page above the break.</p> <p>Once you do make the jump, you may have trouble focusing on the text in the image below. That&#8217;s because of a phenomenon known as <em>chromostereopsis</em>, which the American National Standard Institute (<a href="http://www.hfes.org/Publications/ProductDetail.aspx?Id=76">ANSI/HFES-200, Part 5</a>) defines  as &#8220;the perception of depth resulting from the close proximity of two colors of disparate wavelengths&#8221;. There&#8217;s a good explanation of <a href="http://www.luminanze.com/writings/chromostereopsis_in_ux_design.html" target="_blank"><em>chromostereopsis </em>here</a><em>. </em>Because of where in our eyes the receptors for different colors are, and how our eyes focus, we perceive different colors as being at different distances. Printers and others who do graphic layout have long known that because they are at opposite ends of the spectrum, it&#8217;s not a good idea to use blue letters on red backgrounds and vice versa. Most people perceive blue as closer than red, and as a result the human eye cannot focus on both red and blue at the same time, causing the optical illusion of blurry letters in the graphic below.<span id="more-519881"></span></p> <p><!--more--></p> <p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://www.thetruthaboutcars.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/REDANDBLUELIGHTS.jpg" rel="lightbox[519881]"><img alt="REDANDBLUELIGHTS" src="http://www.thetruthaboutcars.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/REDANDBLUELIGHTS-550x366.jpg" width="550" height="366" /></a></p> <p>I apologize for for the eye strain but I was literally trying to illustrate a point. It could have been worse, I could have made it a flashing, animated GIF.  To remove that visual abomination, click on read more.<!--more--></p> <p>Isn&#8217;t that better?</p> <p>Back to the topic.</p> <p>In addition to  <em>chromostereopsis, </em>as LEDs have proliferated, people have come to realize that its harder to focus on pure blue lights than on any other color. Our retinal receptors are known as rods and cones. Visual acuity comes from rods and is mostly a black and white phenomenon. Color is added by cone receptors. Rods are sensitive mostly to light in the yellow-green part of the spectrum. Pure blue light doesn&#8217;t activate rods sufficiently for clear vision.</p> <p>Flashing blue lights make it hard to focus but flashing red and blue lights together is an even worse idea. To begin with it makes it hard to estimate the distance of an emergency with flashing red and blue lights. More dangerously, when your visual system is being flooded simultaneously with bright red and blue lights, the effect is almost blinding, certainly visually confusing. It&#8217;s a problem for motorists but it seems to me it would create an even more dangerous situation for police officers who have to make out shapes and distances in visually confusing lighting situations.</p> <p>So why do police cars use blue lights in the first place and even worse, red and blue lights together? I suspect the reason is partly historical. In some states police used red lights and in others blue lights. It made sense for manufacturers to offer units with both colors. However, I think the main reason is exactly <em>chromostereopsis.</em> I think the companies selling emergency lights have to be aware of the phenomenon, and they wanted to come up with lights that would surely get your attention. Does the sign above get the attention of your visual system?</p> <p>A while back I contacted a handful of companies that manufacture and supply emergency lights to police agencies abpuit <em>chromostereopsis </em>but none would comment. It&#8217;s not as though the phenomenon is not well known. Just about everyone who works with color knows not to do two-tone with red and blue.</p> <p>So why do police cars do use red and blue flashing lights?</p> <p><em>Ronnie Schreiber edits <a href="http://www.carsindepth.com/" target="_blank"><strong>Cars In Depth</strong></a>, a realistic perspective on cars &amp; car culture and the original 3D car site.
