Behavior in the Human Male</i>, the first of the two Kinsey reports.</div> </div> </div> <p>The <b>Kinsey Reports</b> are two books on <a href="/wiki/Human_sexual_behavior" title="Human sexual behavior" class="mw-redirect">human sexual behavior</a>: <i><b>Sexual Behavior in the Human Male</b></i><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> (1948) and <i><b>Sexual Behavior in the Human Female</b></i><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> (1953), written by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Kinsey" title="Alfred Kinsey">Alfred Kinsey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wardell_Pomeroy" title="Wardell Pomeroy">Wardell Pomeroy</a> and others and published by <a href="/wiki/Saunders_(publisher)" title="Saunders (publisher)" class="mw-redirect">Saunders</a>. Kinsey was a <a href="/wiki/Zoologist" title="Zoologist" class="mw-redirect">zoologist</a> at <a href="/wiki/Indiana_University_(Bloomington)" title="Indiana University (Bloomington)" class="mw-redirect">Indiana University</a> and the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Kinsey_Institute_for_Research_in_Sex,_Gender_and_Reproduction" title="Kinsey Institute for Research in Sex, Gender and Reproduction" class="mw-redirect">Kinsey Institute for Research in Sex, Gender and Reproduction</a> (more widely known as the Kinsey Institute).</p> <p><i>Sexual Behavior in the Human Female</i> was based on personal interviews with approximately 6,000 women. Kinsey analyzed data for the frequency with which women participate in various types of sexual activity and looked at how factors such as age, social-economic status and religious adherence influence sexual behavior. Comparisons are made of female and male sexual activities. Kinsey's evidence suggested that women were less sexually active than men.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>The publications were immediately controversial among the general public. The findings caused shock and outrage, both because they challenged conventional beliefs about <a href="/wiki/Human_sexuality" title="Human sexuality">sexuality</a> and because they discussed subjects that had previously been <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>Kinsey's methodology used to collect data has received criticism. It has been suggested that some data in the reports could not have been obtained without collaborations with child molesters.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The Kinsey Institute denies this charge, though it acknowledges that men who have had sexual experiences with children were interviewed, with Kinsey balancing what he saw as the need for their anonymity to solicit "honest answers on such taboo subjects" against the likelihood that their crimes would continue.<sup id="cite_ref-kinsey-inst_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kinsey-inst-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Welsh-Huggins_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Welsh-Huggins-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Additionally, concerns over the sample populations used were later addressed by the Kinsey Institute. The conclusion of the Kinsey Institute was that none of Kinsey's original estimates were significantly affected by these data sources.<sup id="cite_ref-MDOG_8-0" class="reference"><a experience.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> 11.6% of white males (ages 20–35) were given a rating of 3 (about equal heterosexual href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kinsey_Reports&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> <p>Academic criticisms were made pertaining to sample selection and sample bias in the reports' <a href="/wiki/Methodology" title="Methodology">methodology</a>. Two main problems cited were that significant portions of the samples come from prison populations and male prostitutes, and that people who volunteer to be interviewed about <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboo</a> subject are likely to suffer from the problem of <a href="/wiki/Self-selection" title="Self-selection" class="mw-redirect">self-selection</a>. Both undermine the usefulness of the sample in terms of determining the tendencies of the overall population. In 1948, the same year as the original publication, a committee of the <a href="/wiki/American_Statistical_Association" title="American Statistical Association">American Statistical Association</a>, including notable statisticians such as <a href="/wiki/John_Tukey" title="John Tukey">John Tukey</a>, condemned the sampling procedure. Tukey was perhaps the most vocal critic, saying, "A random selection of three people would have been better than a group of 300 chosen by Mr. Kinsey."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Criticism principally revolved around the over-representation of some groups in the sample: 25% were, or had been, <a href="/wiki/Prison" title="Prison">prison</a> inmates, and 5% were <a href="/wiki/Male_prostitute" title="Male prostitute" class="mw-redirect">male prostitutes</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Psychologist" title="Psychologist">Psychologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Maslow" title="Abraham Maslow">Abraham Maslow</a> asserted that Kinsey did not consider "<a href="/wiki/Volunteer_bias" title="Volunteer bias" class="mw-redirect">volunteer bias</a>". The data represented only those volunteering to participate in discussion of taboo topics. Most Americans were reluctant to discuss the intimate details of their sex lives even with their spouses and close friends. Before the publication of Kinsey's reports, Maslow tested Kinsey's volunteers for bias. He concluded that Kinsey's sample was unrepresentative of the general population.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1954, leading statisticians, including <a href="/wiki/William_Gemmell_Cochran" title="William Gemmell Cochran">William Gemmell Cochran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Mosteller" title="Frederick Mosteller">Frederick Mosteller</a>, John Tukey, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=W._O._Jenkins&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="W. O. Jenkins (page does not exist)">W. O. Further, the conclusions drawn from data presented in the book are often stated by KPM [Kinsey, Pomeroy, and Martin] in much too bold and confident a manner. Taken cumulatively, these objections amount to saying that much of the writing in the book falls below the level of good scientific writing.”<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> </blockquote> <p>In response, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gebhard" title="Paul Gebhard">Paul Gebhard</a>, Kinsey's successor as director of the <a href="/wiki/Kinsey_Institute_for_Sex_Research" title="Kinsey Institute for Sex Research" class="mw-redirect">Kinsey Institute for Sex Research</a>, cleaned the Kinsey data of purported contaminants,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. Johnson) published <i>The Kinsey Data: Marginal Tabulations of the 1938–1963 Interviews Conducted by the Institute for Sex Research</i>. Their conclusion, to Gebhard's surprise he claimed, was that none of Kinsey's original estimates were significantly affected by this bias: that is, the prison population and male prostitutes had the same statistical tendency as those who willingly participated in discussion of previously taboo sexual topics. The scale ranges from 0 to 6, with 0 being completely heterosexual and 6 completely homosexual. An additional category, X, was mentioned to describe those who had "no socio-sexual contacts or reactions,"<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> which has been cited by scholars to mean <a href="/wiki/Asexuality" title="Asexuality">asexuality</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stange_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stange-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The scale was first published in <i>Sexual Behavior in the Human Male</i> (1948) by Kinsey, <a href="/wiki/Wardell_Pomeroy" title="Wardell Pomeroy">Wardell Pomeroy</a> and others, and was also prominent in the complementary work <i>Sexual Behavior in the Human Female</i> (1953). An individual may be assigned a position on this scale, for each period in his life. [...] A seven-point scale comes nearer to showing the many gradations that actually exist.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
