<p><b>Godwin's law</b> (or <b>Godwin's Rule of Nazi Analogies</b>)<sup id="cite_ref-GL_FAQ_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GL_FAQ-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Godwin94_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Godwin94-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> is an <a href="/wiki/Internet_humor" title="Internet humor">Internet adage</a> asserting that "As an online discussion grows longer, the probability of a comparison involving Nazis or Hitler <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Almost_surely" title="Almost surely">approaches 1</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-Godwin94_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Godwin94-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Godwin95canonical_version_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Godwin95canonical_version-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup>—​</span> that is, if an online discussion (regardless of topic or scope) goes on long enough, sooner or later someone will compare someone or something to <a href="/wiki/Hitler" title="Hitler" class="mw-redirect">Hitler</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazism</a>.</p> <p>Promulgated by American attorney and author <a href="/wiki/Mike_Godwin" title="Mike Godwin">Mike Godwin</a> in 1990,<sup id="cite_ref-Godwin94_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Godwin94-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Godwin's Law originally referred, specifically, to <a href="/wiki/Usenet" title="Usenet">Usenet</a> <a href="/wiki/Newsgroup" title="Newsgroup" class="mw-redirect">newsgroup</a> discussions.<sup id="cite_ref-Godwin_1991_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Godwin_1991-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> It is now applied to any <a href="/wiki/Conversation_threading" title="Conversation threading">threaded online discussion</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Internet_forum" title="Internet forum">Internet forums</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chat_room" title="Chat room">chat rooms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blog</a> comment threads, as well as to speeches, articles and other rhetoric.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldacre_2010_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldacre_2010-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hillary_Putin_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hillary_Putin-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>In 2012, "Godwin's Law" became an entry in the third edition of the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p></p> <div id="toc" class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li> </ul> </div> <p></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corollaries_and_usage">Corollaries and usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Godwin%27s_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Corollaries and usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>There are many <a href="/wiki/Corollary" title="Corollary">corollaries</a> to Godwin's law, some considered more <a href="/wiki/Canon_(basic_principle)" title="Canon (basic principle)">canonical</a> (by being adopted by Godwin himself)<sup id="cite_ref-Godwin95canonical_version_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Godwin95canonical_version-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> than others.<sup id="cite_ref-GL_FAQ_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GL_FAQ-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> For example, there is a tradition in many newsgroups and other Internet discussion forums that once such a comparison is made, the thread is finished and whoever mentioned the Nazis has automatically lost whatever <a href="/wiki/Debate" title="Debate">debate</a> was in progress.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> This principle is itself frequently referred to as Godwin's law. It is considered poor form to raise such a comparison arbitrarily with the motive of ending the thread.</p> <p>Godwin's law applies especially to inappropriate, inordinate, or <a href="/wiki/Hyperbole" title="Hyperbole">hyperbolic comparisons</a> of other situations (or one's opponent) with Nazis – often referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Reductio_ad_Hitlerum" title="Reductio ad Hitlerum">playing the Hitler card</a>". The law and its corollaries would not apply to discussions covering known mainstays of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eugenics" title="Eugenics">eugenics</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Racial_superiority" title="Racial superiority" class="mw-redirect">racial superiority</a>, nor, more debatably, to a discussion of other <a href="/wiki/Totalitarianism" title="Totalitarianism">totalitarian regimes</a> or ideologies<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, if that was the explicit topic of conversation, because a Nazi comparison in those circumstances may be appropriate, in effect committing the <a href="/wiki/Argument_from_fallacy" title="Argument from fallacy">fallacist's fallacy</a>. Whether it applies to humorous use or references to oneself is open to interpretation, because this would not be a fallacious attack against a debate opponent.</p> <p>Although falling foul of Godwin's law tends to cause the individual making the comparison to lose his argument or credibility, Godwin's law itself can be abused as a distraction, diversion or even as <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">censorship</a>, fallaciously miscasting an opponent's argument as <a href="/wiki/Hyperbole" title="Hyperbole">hyperbole</a> when the comparisons made by the argument are actually appropriate.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Similar criticisms of the "law" (or "at least the distorted version which purports to prohibit all comparisons to German crimes") have been made by <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Greenwald" title="Glenn Greenwald">Glenn Greenwald</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Godwin%27s_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>Godwin has stated that he introduced Godwin's law in 1990 as an experiment in <a href="/wiki/Memetics" title="Memetics">memetics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Godwin94_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Godwin94-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>Godwin's law does not claim to articulate a fallacy; it is instead framed as a memetic tool to reduce the incidence of inappropriate hyperbolic comparisons. "Although deliberately framed as if it were a law of nature or of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, its purpose has always been <a href="/wiki/Rhetorics" title="Rhetorics" class="mw-redirect">rhetorical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pedagogy" title="Pedagogy">pedagogical</a>: I wanted folks who glibly compared someone else to Hitler or to Nazis to think a bit harder about <a href="/wiki/The_Holocaust" title="The Holocaust">the Holocaust</a>", Godwin has written.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
