class="firstHeading" lang="en"><span dir="auto">Eternal September</span></h1>       <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">          <div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>         <div id="contentSub"></div>             <div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">      Jump to:     <a href="#mw-navigation">navigation</a>,      <a href="#p-search">search</a>     </div>     <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Usenet" title="Usenet">Usenet</a> slang, <b>Eternal September</b> (or the <b>September that never ended</b>)<sup id="cite_ref-jargon_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jargon-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> began in September 1993,<sup id="cite_ref-AutoOO-1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AutoOO-1-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> the month that Internet service provider <a href="/wiki/AOL" title="AOL">America Online</a> began offering Usenet access to its tens of thousands, and later millions, of users. Before then, every year in September, a large number of new university <a href="/wiki/Freshmen" title="Freshmen" class="mw-redirect">freshmen</a> acquired access to Usenet for the first time, and took some time to become accustomed to Usenet's standards of conduct and "<a href="/wiki/Netiquette" title="Netiquette" class="mw-redirect">netiquette</a>". But, after a month or so, these new users would learn the networks' <a href="/wiki/Social_norms" title="Social norms" class="mw-redirect">social norms</a> or simply tire of using the service. However, for the pre-existing users of <a href="/wiki/Usenet" title="Usenet">Usenet</a>, the influx of new users from September 1993 onwards was a new and endless manifestation of the phenomenon.</p> <p>This was in part because the service providers (such as AOL) made little effort to educate their users about Usenet customs, but it was also because of the large scale and seemingly endless intake of new users. Whereas the regular September freshman influx would quickly settle down, Usenet's existing culture did not have the capacity to integrate the sheer and endless number of new users and so they overwhelmed the network's existing <a href="/wiki/Social_norm" title="Social norm" class="mw-redirect">social norms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AutoOO-3_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AutoOO-3-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>Since then, the rise in popularity of the Internet has brought on a constant stream of new users. Thus, from the point of view of the pre-1993 Usenet users, the influx of new users in September 1993 never ended. The term was used by Dave Fischer in a January 26, 1994, post to alt.folklore.computers, "It's moot now. September 1993 will go down in net.history as the September that never ended."<sup id="cite_ref-firstuse_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-firstuse-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>In homage to the term, one <a href="/wiki/News_server" title="News server">news server</a>, formerly named <i>Motzarella.org</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-freag.net_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freag.net-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> now calls itself <i>Eternal September</i> and gives the date as a running tally of days since September 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-AutoOO-2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AutoOO-2-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>A <a href="/wiki/Tongue-in-cheek" title="Tongue-in-cheek">tongue-in-cheek</a> program called <b>sdate</b> outputs the current date, formatted using the Eternal September calendar (September <i>X</i>, 1993, with <i>X</i> an unbounded counter for days since the beginning of that <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(reference_date)" title="Epoch (reference date)">epoch</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> This should not be confused with the identically named <i>sdate</i>, one of the sixty <a href="/wiki/Command_(computing)" title="Command (computing)">commands</a> that came with the First Edition of <a href="/wiki/Unix" title="Unix">Unix</a>, that was used to set the <a href="/wiki/System_clock" title="System clock" class="mw-redirect">system clock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline"
