href="/wiki/File:Atheism_template.svg" class="image"><img alt="Portal icon" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Atheism_template.svg/16px-Atheism_template.svg.png" width="16" style="text-align:right;font-size:115%;padding-top: 0.6em;;"> <div class="plainlinks hlist navbar mini"> <ul> <li class="nv-view"><a href="/wiki/Template:Atheism_sidebar" title="Template:Atheism template" style=";">t</span></a></li> <li class="nv-edit"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Template:Atheism_sidebar&amp;action=edit"><span title="Edit this template" style=";">e</span></a></li> </ul> </div> </td> </tr> </table> <p>The <b>omnipotence paradox</b> is a family of semantic <a href="/wiki/Paradox" title="Paradox">paradoxes</a> which address two general issues and three specific issues: <i>Is an <a href="/wiki/Omnipotent" title="Omnipotent" class="mw-redirect">omnipotent</a> entity logically possible?</i> and <i>What do we mean by 'omnipotence'?</i>; and <i>What do we mean by 'power'?</i>, <i>What do we mean by 'logic'?</i>, and <i>What is the relation between power and logic?</i>.</p> <p>The omnipotence paradox states that: <i>If a being can perform any action, then it should be able to create a task which this being is unable to perform; hence, this being cannot perform all actions. Yet, on the other hand, if this being cannot create a task that it is unable to perform, then there exists something it cannot do.</i></p> <p>One version of the omnipotence paradox is the so-called <i>paradox of the stone</i>: "Could an omnipotent being create a stone so heavy that even he could not lift it?" If he could lift the rock, then it seems that the being would not have been omnipotent to begin with in that he would have been incapable of creating a heavy enough stone; if he could not lift the stone, then it seems that the being either would never have been omnipotent to begin with or would have ceased to be omnipotent upon his creation of the stone.<sup id="cite_ref-Savage.2C_C._Wade_1967_pp._74_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Savage.2C_C._Wade_1967_pp._74-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>The argument is medieval, dating at least to the 12th century, addressed by <a href="/wiki/Averro%C3%ABs" title="Averroës" class="mw-redirect">Averroës</a> (1126–1198) and later by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TT_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TT-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pseudo-Dionysius_the_Areopagite" title="Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite">Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite</a> (before 532) has a predecessor version of the paradox, asking whether it is possible for God to "deny himself".</p> <p>Several answers to the paradox have been proposed.</p> <p></p> <div id="toc" class="toc"> <div id="toctitle"> <h2>Contents</h2> </div> <ul> <li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Overview"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Overview</span></a></li> <li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Types_of_omnipotence"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> </li> <li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#Other_versions_of_the_paradox"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">Other versions of the paradox</span></a></li> <li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li> <li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-7"><a href="#Notes"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">Notes</span></a></li> <li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-8"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">7</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li> </ul> </div> <p></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omnipotence_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>A common modern version of the omnipotence paradox is expressed in the question: "<i>Can [an omnipotent being] create a stone so heavy that it cannot lift it?</i>" This question generates a dilemma. The being can either create a stone which it cannot lift, or it cannot create a stone which it cannot lift. If the being <i>can</i> create a stone that it cannot lift, then it seems that it can cease to be omnipotent. If the being <i>cannot</i> create a stone which it cannot lift, then it seems it is already not omnipotent.<sup id="cite_ref-Savage.2C_C._Wade_1967_pp._74_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Savage.2C_C._Wade_1967_pp._74-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup>impossible. But, if the question is inherently required by the concept of omnipotence, then it seems the logic which allows it to be inherently required is a paradox since the particular concept of omnipotence which requires it is a paradox. In short, the act of seeming to find omnipotence to be a contradiction-of-terms is founded on the act of conceiving something against which to construct the contradiction: prior to any ‘act’, omnipotence is conceived as coherent both with itself and with the possibility of knowledge (which begs the question of what is the knowledge that constitutes the identifiability of omnipotence-as-a-paradox?).</p> <p>But, whether the concept of omnipotence itself is a material paradox, or is simply too obscure to us to preclude being construed by paradoxical thinking, the central issue of the <i>omnipotence paradox</i> is whether the concept of the 'logically possible' is different for a world in which omnipotence exists from a world in which omnipotence does not exist. The reason this is the central issue is because our sense of material paradox, and of the logical contradiction of which material paradox is an expression, are functions of the fact that we presuppose that there must be something which exists which is inherently meaningful or logical, that is, which is <i>concretely</i> not a compound of other things or other concepts. So, for example, in a world in which exists a materially paradoxical omnipotence, its very paradoxicality seems either to be a material-paradox-of-a-material-paradox, or to be a non-paradox per the proposition that it exists (i.e., if it exists, then nothing has inherent meaning, including itself). Whereas, a world in which exists non-paradoxical omnipotence, its own omnipotence is coextensive with whatever is the concrete basis of our presupposition that something must be inherently meaningful.</p> <p>The dilemma of omnipotence is similar to another classic paradox, the <a href="/wiki/Irresistible_force_paradox" title="Irresistible force paradox">irresistible force paradox</a>: <i>What happens when an irresistible force meets an immovable object?</i> One response to this paradox is that if a force is irresistible, then, by definition, there is no truly immovable object; conversely, if an immovable object were to exist, then no force could be defined as being truly irresistible. Some claim that the only way out of this paradox is if the irresistible force and the immovable object never meet. But, this way out is not possible in the omnipotence case, because the purpose is to ask if the being's own inherent omnipotence makes its own inherent omnipotence impossible. Moreover, an object cannot in principle be immovable, if there is a force which may move it, regardless of whether the force and the object never meet. So, while, prior to any task, it is easy to imagine that omnipotence is in state of coherence with itself, some imaginable tasks are not possible for such a coherent omnipotence to perform without compromising its coherence.</p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_omnipotence">Types of omnipotence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omnipotence_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of omnipotence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Omnipotence" title="Omnipotence">Omnipotence</a></div> <p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Geach" title="Peter Geach">Peter Geach</a> describes and rejects four levels of omnipotence. He also defines and defends a lesser notion of the "almightiness" of God.</p> <ol> <li>"Y is <b>absolutely omnipotent</b>" means that "Y" can do everything absolutely. Everything that can be expressed in a string of words even if it can be shown to be self-<a href="/wiki/Contradiction" title="Contradiction">contradictory</a>, "Y"is not bound in action, as we are in thought by the laws of logic."<sup id="cite_ref-Geach_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geach-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> This position is advanced by <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="René Descartes">Descartes</a>. It has the theological advantage of making God prior to the laws of logic. Some claim that it in addition gives rise to the theological disadvantage of making God's promises suspect. On this account, the omnipotence paradox is a genuine paradox, but genuine paradoxes might nonetheless be so.</li> <li>"Y is <b>omnipotent</b>" means "Y can do X" is true <a href="/wiki/If_and_only_if" title="If and only if">if and only if</a> X is a logically consistent description of a state of affairs. This position was once advocated by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> This definition of omnipotence solves some of the paradoxes associated with omnipotence, but some modern formulations of the paradox still work against this definition. Let X = "to make something that its maker cannot lift". As <a href="/wiki/George_I._Mavrodes" title="George I. Mavrodes">Mavrodes</a> points out there is nothing logically contradictory about this; a man could, for example, make a boat which he could not lift.<sup id="cite_ref-Mavrodes_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mavrodes-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> It would be strange if humans could accomplish this feat, but an omnipotent being could not. Additionally, this definition has problems when X is morally or physically untenable for a being like God. But this brings about a new problem that if God is bound by logic he therefore cannot be the author of logic.</li> <li>"Y is <b>omnipotent</b>" means "Y can do X" is true if and only if "Y does X" is logically consistent. Here the idea is to exclude actions which would be inconsistent for Y to do but might be consistent for others. Again sometimes it looks as if Aquinas takes this position.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Here Mavrodes' worry about X= "to make something its maker cannot lift" will no longer be a problem because "God does X" is not logically consistent. However, this account may still have problems with moral issues like X = "tells a lie" or temporal issues like X = "brings it about that Rome was never founded."<sup id="cite_ref-Geach_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geach-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></li> <li>"Y is <b>omnipotent</b>" means whenever "Y will bring about X" is logically possible, then "Y can bring about X" is true. This sense, also does not allow the paradox of omnipotence to arise, and unlike definition #3 avoids any temporal worries about whether or not an omnipotent being could change the past. However, Geach criticizes even this sense of omnipotence as misunderstanding the nature of God's promises.<sup id="cite_ref-Geach_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geach-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></li> <li>"Y is <b>almighty</b>" means that Y is not just more powerful than any creature; no creature can compete with Y in power, even unsuccessfully.<sup id="cite_ref-Geach_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geach-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> In this account nothing like the omnipotence paradox arises, but perhaps that is because God is not taken to be in any sense omnipotent. On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Anselm_of_Canterbury" title="Anselm of Canterbury">Anselm of Canterbury</a> seems to think that almightiness is one of the things that make God count as omnipotent.<sup id="cite_ref-amselm_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amselm-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></li> </ol> <p><a href="/wiki/St_Augustine" title="St Augustine" class="mw-redirect">St Augustine</a> in his <a href="/wiki/City_of_God_(book)" title="City of God (book)">City of God</a> writes "<a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> is called omnipotent on account of His doing what He wills" and thus proposes the definition that "Y is <b>omnipotent</b>" means "If Y wishes to do X then Y can and does do X".</p> <p>The notion of omnipotence can also be applied to an entity in different ways. An <b>essentially omnipotent</b> being is an entity that is necessarily omnipotent. In contrast, an <b>accidentally omnipotent</b> being is an entity that can be omnipotent for a temporary period of time, and then becomes non-omnipotent. The omnipotence paradox can be applied to each type of being differently.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoffman_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoffman-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>Some Philosophers, such as <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="René Descartes">René Descartes</a>, argue that God is absolutely omnipotent.<sup id="cite_ref-Descartes.2C_Rene_1641_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Descartes.2C_Rene_1641-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, some philosophers have considered the assumption that a being is either omnipotent or non-omnipotent to be a <a href="/wiki/False_dilemma" title="False dilemma">false dilemma</a>, as it neglects the possibility of varying degrees of omnipotence.<sup id="cite_ref-Haeckel1_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haeckel1-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Some modern approaches to the problem have involved <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantic</a> debates over whether language—and therefore philosophy—can meaningfully address the concept of omnipotence itself.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_answers">Proposed answers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omnipotence_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Proposed answers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>A common response from Christian philosophers, such as <a href="/wiki/Norman_Geisler" title="Norman Geisler">Norman Geisler</a> or <a href="/wiki/Richard_Swinburne" title="Richard Swinburne">Richard Swinburne</a> is that the paradox assumes a wrong definition of omnipotence. Omnipotence, they say, does not mean that God can do anything <i>at all</i> but, rather, that he can do <i>anything that's possible according to his nature.</i> The distinction is important. God cannot perform logical absurdities; he cannot, for instance, make 1+1=3. Likewise, God cannot make a being greater than himself because he is, by definition, the greatest possible being. God is limited in his actions to his nature. The Bible supports this, they assert, in passages such as Hebrews 6:18 which says it is "impossible for God to lie." This raises the question, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Euthyphro_Dilemma" title="Euthyphro Dilemma" class="mw-redirect">Euthyphro Dilemma</a>, of where this law of logic, which God is bound to obey, comes from. According to these theologians, this law is not a law above God that he assents to but, rather, logic is an eternal part of God's nature, like his <a href="/wiki/Omniscience" title="Omniscience">omniscience</a> or <a href="/wiki/Omnibenevolence" title="Omnibenevolence">omnibenevolence</a>. God obeys the laws of logic because God is eternally logical in the same way that God does not perform evil actions because God is eternally good. So, God, by nature logical and unable to violate the laws of logic, cannot make a boulder so heavy he cannot lift it because that would violate the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_non_contradiction" title="Law of non contradiction" class="mw-redirect">law of non contradiction</a> by creating an immovable object and an unstoppable force.</p> <p>Another common response is that since God is supposedly omnipotent, the phrase "could not lift" does not make sense and the paradox is meaningless.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> This may mean that the complexity involved in rightly understanding omnipotence---contra all the logical details involved in misunderstanding it---is a function of the fact that omnipotence, like infinity, is perceived at all by contrasting reference to those complex and variable things <i>which it is not</i>. But, an alternative meaning is that a non-corporeal God cannot lift anything, but can raise it (a linguistic pedantry) - or to use the beliefs of Hindus (that there is one God, who can be manifest as several different beings) that whilst it is possible for God to do all things, it is not possible for all his incarnations to do them. As such, God could create a stone so heavy that, in one incarnation, he was unable to lift it - but would be able to do something that an incarnation that could lift it could not.</p> <p>Other responses claim that the question is sophistry, meaning it makes grammatical sense, but has no intelligible meaning. The lifting a rock paradox (<i>Can God lift a stone larger than he can carry?</i>) uses human characteristics to cover up the main skeletal structure of the question. With these assumptions made, two arguments can stem from it:</p> <ol> <li><b>Lifting</b> covers up the definition of <b>translation</b>, which means moving something from one point in space to another. With this in mind, the real question would be, "<i>Can God move a rock from one location in space to another that is larger than possible?</i>" In order for the rock to not be able to move from one space to another, it would have to be larger than space itself. However, it is impossible for a rock to be larger than space, as space will always adjust itself to cover the space of the rock. If the supposed rock was out of space-time dimension, then the question would not make sense, because it would be impossible to move an object from one location in space to another if there is no space to begin with, meaning the faulting is with the logic of the question and not God's capabilities.</li> <li>The words, "Lift a Stone", are used instead to substitute capability. With this in mind, essentially the question is asking if God is incapable, so the real question would be, "<i>Is God capable of being incapable?</i>" If God is capable of being incapable, it means that He is incapable, because He has the potential to not be able to do something. Conversely, if God is incapable of being incapable, then the two inabilities cancel each other out, making God have the capability to do something.</li> </ol> <p>The act of killing oneself is not applicable to an omnipotent being, since, despite that such an act does involve some power, it also involves a <i>lack</i> of power: the human person who can kill himself is already not indestructible, and, in fact, every agent constituting his environment is more powerful in some ways than himself. In other words, all non-omnipotent agents are concretely <i>synthetic</i>: constructed as contingencies of other, smaller, agents, meaning that they, unlike an omnipotent agent, logically can exist not only in multiple instantiation (by being constructed out of the more basic agents of which they are made), but are each bound to a differentiated location in space contra transcendent <a href="/wiki/Omnipresence" title="Omnipresence">omnipresence</a>.</p> <p><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a> asserts that the paradox arises from a misunderstanding of omnipotence. He maintains that inherent contradictions and logical impossibilities do not fall under the omnipotence of God.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> J. L Cowan sees this paradox as a reason to reject the concept of 'absolute' omnipotence,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> while others, such as <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="René Descartes">René Descartes</a>, argue that God is absolutely omnipotent, despite the problem.<sup id="cite_ref-Descartes.2C_Rene_1641_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Descartes.2C_Rene_1641-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p><a href="/wiki/C._S._Lewis" title="C. S. Lewis">C. S. Lewis</a> argues that when talking about omnipotence, referencing "a rock so heavy that God cannot lift it" is nonsense just as much as referencing "a square circle"; that it is not logically coherent in terms of power to think that omnipotence includes the power to do the logically impossible. So asking "Can God create a rock so heavy that even he cannot lift it?" is just as much nonsense as asking "Can God draw a square circle?" The logical contradiction here being God's simultaneous ability and disability in lifting the rock: the statement "God can lift this rock" must have a truth value of either true or false, it cannot possess both. This is justified by observing that in order for the omnipotent agent to create such a stone, the omnipotent agent must already be more powerful than itself: such a stone is too heavy for the omnipotent agent to lift, but the omnipotent agent already can create such a stone; If an omnipotent agent already is more powerful than itself, then it already is just that powerful. Which means that its power to create a stone that’s too heavy for it to lift is identical to its power to lift that very stone. While this doesn’t quite make complete sense, Lewis wished to stress its implicit point: that even within the attempt to prove that the concept of omnipotence is immediately incoherent, one admits that it is immediately coherent, and that the only difference is that this attempt if forced to admit this despite that the attempt is constituted by a perfectly irrational route to its own unwilling end, with a perfectly irrational set of 'things' included in that end.</p> <p>In other words, that the 'limit' on what omnipotence 'can' do is not a <i>limit</i> on its actual agency, but an <i>epistemological boundary</i> without which omnipotence could not be <i>identified</i> (paradoxically or otherwise) in the first place. In fact, this process is merely a fancier form of the classic <a href="/wiki/Liar_Paradox" title="Liar Paradox" class="mw-redirect">Liar Paradox</a>: If I say, "I am a liar", then how can it be true if I am telling the truth therewith, and, if I am telling the truth therewith, then how can I be a liar? So, to think that omnipotence is an <i>epistemological</i> paradox is like failing to recognize that, when taking the statement, 'I am a liar' self-referentially, the statement is reduced to an actual failure to lie. In other words, if one maintains the supposedly 'initial' position that the necessary conception of omnipotence includes the 'power' to compromise both itself and all other identity, and if one concludes from this position that omnipotence is epistemologically incoherent, then one implicitly is asserting that one's own 'initial' position is incoherent. Therefore the question (and therefore the perceived paradox) is meaningless. Nonsense does not suddenly acquire sense and meaning with the addition of the two words, "God can" before it.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Lewis additionally said that "unless something is self-evident, nothing can be proved", which implies for the debate on omnipotence that, <i>as in matter, so in the human understanding of truth: it takes no true insight to destroy a perfectly integrated structure, and the effort to destroy has greater effect than an equal effort to build; so, a man is thought a fool who assumes its integrity, and thought an abomination who argues for it. It is easier to teach a fish to swim in outer space than to convince a room full of ignorant fools why it cannot be done.</i></p> <p><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Christian_Uy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John Christian Uy (page does not exist)">John Christian Uy</a> said that it is just the same as someone with double-bladed sword (accidentally omnipotent), or sword and a shield (essentially omnipotent). Therefore, an accidentally omnipotent deity CAN remove its omnipotence while an essentially omnipotent deity CANNOT do anything that would make it non-omnipotent. Both however, have no limitations so far other than the essential omnipotent being who cannot do anything which will make it non-omnipotent like making someone equal with him, lowering or improving himself (for omnipotence is the highest) etc. It could, however, make someone with a great power, though it cannot be 99% because Omnipotence is infinite, because that created being is not equal with him. Overall, God in the Christian Bible, is essentially omnipotent.</p> <p>In a 1955 article published in the philosophy journal <i>Mind</i>, <a href="/wiki/J._L._Mackie" title="J. L. Mackie">J. L. Mackie</a> attempted to resolve the paradox by distinguishing between first-order omnipotence (unlimited power to act) and second-order omnipotence (unlimited power to determine what powers to act things shall have).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> An omnipotent being with both first and second-order omnipotence at a particular time might restrict its own power to act and, henceforth, cease to be omnipotent in either sense. There has been considerable philosophical dispute since Mackie, as to the best way to formulate the paradox of omnipotence in formal logic.<sup id="cite_ref-TPoG_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TPoG-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>Another common response to the omnipotence paradox is to try to define omnipotence to mean something weaker than absolute omnipotence, such as definition 3 or 4 above. The paradox can be resolved by simply stipulating that omnipotence does not require the being to have abilities which are logically impossible, but only to be able to do anything which conforms to the laws of logic. A good example of a modern defender of this line of reasoning is George Mavrodes.<sup id="cite_ref-Mavrodes_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mavrodes-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Essentially, Mavrodes argues that it is no limitation on a being's omnipotence to say that it cannot make a round square. Such a "task" is termed by him a "pseudo-task" as it is self-contradictory and inherently nonsense. <a href="/wiki/Harry_Frankfurt" title="Harry Frankfurt">Harry Frankfurt</a>—following from Descartes—has responded to this solution with a proposal of his own: that God can create a stone impossible to lift and also lift said stone</p> <blockquote> <p>For why should God not be able to perform the task in question? To be sure, it is a task—the task of lifting a stone which He cannot lift—whose description is self-contradictory. But if God is supposed capable of performing one task whose description is self-contradictory—that of creating the problematic stone in the first place—why should He not be supposed capable of performing another—that of lifting the stone? After all, is there any greater trick in performing two logically impossible tasks than there is in performing one?<sup id="cite_ref-frankfurt_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frankfurt-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> </blockquote> <p>If a being is <b>accidentally omnipotent</b>, then it can resolve the paradox by creating a stone which it cannot lift and thereby becoming non-omnipotent. Unlike essentially omnipotent entities, it is possible for an accidentally omnipotent being to be non-omnipotent. This raises the question, however, of whether or not the being was ever truly omnipotent, or just capable of great power.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoffman_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoffman-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> On the other hand, the ability to voluntarily give up great power is often thought of as central to the notion of the Christian Incarnation.<sup id="cite_ref-gore_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gore-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>If a being is <b>essentially omnipotent</b>, then it can also resolve the paradox (as long as we take omnipotence not to require absolute omnipotence). The omnipotent being is essentially omnipotent, and therefore it is impossible for it to be non-omnipotent. Further, the omnipotent being can do what is logically impossible and have no limitations just like the accidentally omnipotent but the ability to make oneself non-omnipotent. The creation of a stone which the omnipotent being cannot lift would be an impossibility. The omnipotent being cannot create such a stone because its power will be equal to him and thus, remove his omnipotence for there can only be one omnipotent being in existence, but nevertheless retains its omnipotence. This solution works even with definition 2, as long as we also know the being is essentially omnipotent rather than accidentally so. However, it is possible for non-omnipotent beings to compromise their own powers, which presents the paradox that non-omnipotent beings can do something (to themselves) which an essentially omnipotent being cannot do (to itself).</p> <p>This was essentially the position taken by <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Augustine of Hippo</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/City_of_God_(book)" title="City of God (book)">The City of God</a></i>:</p> <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px; display:table;"> <p>For He is called omnipotent on account of His doing what He wills, not on account of His suffering what He wills not; for if that should befall Him, He would by no means be omnipotent. Wherefore, He cannot do some things for the very reason that He is omnipotent.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p> </blockquote> <p>Thus Augustine argued that God could not do anything or create any situation that would in effect make God not God.</p> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_and_omnipotence">Language and omnipotence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omnipotence_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Language and omnipotence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>The philosopher <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Wittgenstein" title="Ludwig Wittgenstein">Ludwig Wittgenstein</a> is often interpreted as arguing that language is not up to the task of describing the kind of power an omnipotent being would have. In his <i><a href="/wiki/Tractatus_Logico-Philosophicus" title="Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus">Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus</a></i> he stays generally within the realm of <a href="/wiki/Logical_positivism" title="Logical positivism">logical positivism</a>, until claim 6.4, but at 6.41 and following the succeeding propositions argue that <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a> and several other issues are "transcendental" subjects which we cannot examine with language. Wittgenstein also mentions the will, life after death, and God; arguing that "When the answer cannot be put into words, neither can the question be put into words".<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>Wittgenstein's work makes the omnipotence paradox a problem in <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantics</a>, the study of how symbols are given meaning. (The retort "That's only semantics" is a way of saying that a statement only concerns the definitions of words, instead of anything important in the physical world.) According to the <i>Tractatus,</i> then, even attempting to formulate the omnipotence paradox is futile, since language cannot refer to the entities the paradox considers. The final proposition of the <i>Tractatus</i> gives Wittgenstein's dictum for these circumstances: "What we cannot speak of, we must pass over in silence".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Wittgenstein's approach to these problems is influential among other 20th century religious thinkers such as <a href="/wiki/D._Z._Phillips" title="D. Z. Phillips" class="mw-redirect">D. Z. Phillips</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-phillips_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phillips-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>But in his later years, Wittgenstein wrote works which are often interpreted as conflicting with his positions in the <i>Tractatus</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-hacker_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hacker-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> and indeed the later Wittgenstein is mainly seen as the leading critic of the early Wittgenstein.</p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_versions_of_the_paradox">Other versions of the paradox</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omnipotence_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other versions of the paradox">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>In the 6th century, <a href="/wiki/Pseudo-Dionysius" title="Pseudo-Dionysius" class="mw-redirect">Pseudo-Dionysius</a> claims that a version of the omnipotence paradox constituted the dispute between St. Paul and Elymas the Magician mentioned in Acts 13:8, but it is phrased in terms of a debate as to whether or not God can "deny himself" ala 2 Tim 2:13.<sup id="cite_ref-psd_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-psd-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> In the 11th century, St. <a href="/wiki/Anselm_of_Canterbury" title="Anselm of Canterbury">Anselm</a> argues that there are many things that God cannot do, but that nonetheless he counts as omnipotent.<sup id="cite_ref-anselm_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anselm-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a> advanced a version of the omnipotence paradox by asking whether God could create a triangle with internal angles that did not add up to 180 degrees. As Aquinas put it in <a href="/wiki/Summa_contra_Gentiles" title="Summa contra Gentiles">Summa contra Gentiles</a>:</p> <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px; display:table;"> <p>Since the principles of certain sciences, such as logic, geometry and arithmetic are taken only from the formal principles of things, on which the essence of the thing depends, it follows that God could not make things contrary to these principles. For example, that a genus was not predicable of the species, or that lines drawn from the centre to the circumference were not equal, or that a triangle did not have three angles equal to two right angles.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p> </blockquote> <p>This can be done on a sphere, and not on a flat surface. The later invention of <a href="/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry" title="Non-Euclidean geometry">non-Euclidean geometry</a> does not resolve this question; for one might as well ask, "If given the axioms of <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_geometry" title="Elliptic geometry">Riemannian geometry</a>, can an omnipotent being create a triangle whose angles <i>do not</i> add up to <i>more</i> than 180 degrees?" In either case, the real question is whether or not an omnipotent being would have the ability to evade the consequences which follow logically from a system of axioms that the being created.</p> <p>A version of the paradox can also be seen in non-theological contexts. A similar problem occurs when accessing legislative or <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_sovereignty" title="Parliamentary sovereignty">parliamentary sovereignty</a>, which holds a specific legal institution to be omnipotent in legal power, and in particular such an institution's ability to regulate itself.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>In a sense, the classic statement of the omnipotence paradox — a rock so heavy that its omnipotent creator cannot lift it — is grounded in <a href="/wiki/Aristotelianism" title="Aristotelianism">Aristotelian</a> science. After all, if one considers the stone's position relative to the sun around which the planet orbits, one could hold that the stone is <i>constantly</i> being lifted—strained though that interpretation would be in the present context. Modern physics indicates that the choice of phrasing about lifting stones should relate to acceleration; however, this does not in itself of course invalidate the fundamental concept of the generalized omnipotence paradox. However, one could easily modify the classic statement as follows: "An omnipotent being creates a <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> which follows the laws of Aristotelian physics. Within this universe, can the omnipotent being create a stone so heavy that the being cannot lift it?"</p> <p><a href="/wiki/Ethan_Allen" title="Ethan Allen">Ethan Allen</a>'s <i>Reason</i> addresses the topics of <a href="/wiki/Original_sin" title="Original sin">original sin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theodicy" title="Theodicy">theodicy</a> and several others in classic <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> fashion.<sup id="cite_ref-allen_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allen-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> In Chapter 3, section IV, he notes that "omnipotence itself" could not exempt animal life from mortality, since change and death are defining attributes of such life. He argues, "the one cannot be without the other, any more than there could be a compact number of mountains without valleys, or that I could exist and not exist at the same time, or that God should effect any other contradiction in nature." Labeled by his friends a <a href="/wiki/Deism" title="Deism">Deist</a>, Allen accepted the notion of a divine being, though throughout <i>Reason</i> he argues that even a divine being must be circumscribed by logic.</p> <p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Principles_of_Philosophy" title="Principles of Philosophy">Principles of Philosophy</a>,</i> <a href="/wiki/Descartes" title="Descartes" class="mw-redirect">Descartes</a> tried refuting the existence of atoms with a variation of this argument, claiming God could not create things so indivisible that he could not divide them.</p> <p>The paradox also appears in popular culture. In an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">The Simpsons</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Homer_Jay_Simpson" title="Homer Jay Simpson" class="mw-redirect">Homer</a> asks <a href="/wiki/Ned_Flanders" title="Ned Flanders">Ned Flanders</a> the question "Could <a href="/wiki/Jesus_of_Nazareth" title="Jesus of Nazareth" class="mw-redirect">Jesus</a> microwave a burrito so hot that He Himself could not eat it?" In one strip of the webcomic <a href="/wiki/Saturday_Morning_Breakfast_Cereal" title="Saturday Morning Breakfast Cereal">Saturday Morning Breakfast Cereal</a>, a child is seen asking a priest "Could God make an argument so circular that even He couldn't believe it?"</p> <p>In the <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> <a href="/wiki/Runaways_(comics)" title="Runaways (comics)">Runaways</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victor_Mancha" title="Victor Mancha">Victor Mancha</a>, the technorganic <a href="/wiki/Android_(robot)" title="Android (robot)">android</a> created by <a href="/wiki/Ultron" title="Ultron">Ultron</a>, is shown as unable to process correctly paradoxes: as such, it's known that a small number of well known paradoxes may force his logic in a permanent loop, shutting his functions down until someone steps in to give Victor the proper solution. As such, his peers stop him once by asking "Could God make a sandwich so big that even he couldn't finish it?", and reboot his mind by explaining him a simplified version of the <i>God as essentially omnipotent</i> solution ("Yes. God could make a sandwich so big that even he couldn't finish it, and eat it all").</p> <p>In the book <i>Bart Simpson's Guide to Life</i> this question is phrased as, "If God can do anything, could he create a hot dog so big that even he couldn't eat it?"</p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omnipotence_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <ul> <li><a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">Absolute monarchy</a></li> <li><a href="/wiki/Atlas_(mythology)" title="Atlas (mythology)">Atlas</a>, the deity responsible for holding the celestial spheres in Greek mythology</li> <li><a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6del%27s_incompleteness_theorems" title="Gödel's incompleteness theorems">Gödel's incompleteness theorems</a></li> <li><a href="/wiki/Irresistible_force_paradox" title="Irresistible force paradox">Irresistible force paradox</a></li> <li><a href="/wiki/Problem_of_evil" title="Problem of evil">Problem of evil</a></li> <li><a href="/wiki/Russell%27s_paradox" title="Russell's paradox">Russell's paradox</a></li> <li><a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">Semantics</a></li> <li><a href="/wiki/Theodicy" title="Theodicy">Theodicy</a></li> </ul> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Omnipotence_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <div class="reflist columns references-column-count references-column-count-2" style="-moz-column-count: 2;
