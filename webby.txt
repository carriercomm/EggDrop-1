Plant</b> is a facility located in <a href="/wiki/McCracken_County,_Kentucky" title="McCracken County, Kentucky">McCracken County, Kentucky</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Paducah,_Kentucky" title="Paducah, Kentucky">Paducah, Kentucky</a> that produced <a href="/wiki/Enriched_uranium" title="Enriched uranium">enriched uranium</a> 1952-2013. The plant is now operated by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Enrichment_Corporation" title="United States Enrichment Corporation">United States Enrichment Corporation</a>, a subsidiary of USEC Incorporated, a publicly traded corporation (NYSE: USU). It was the only operating uranium enrichment facility in the United States in the period 2001-2010. The Paducah plant produced low-enriched uranium, originally as feedstock for military reactors and weapons refining and later for nuclear power fuel.</p> <p>The gaseous diffusion plant covers 750 acres (300&#160;ha) of a 3,425 acres (1,386&#160;ha) site. The four process buildings cover 74 acres (30&#160;ha), and consume a peak electrical demand of 3,040 megawatts.<sup id="cite_ref-usec2_1-0" class="reference"><a tocsection-9"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">9</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li> </ul> </div> <p></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paducah_Gaseous_Diffusion_Plant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>The former Kentucky Ordnance Works site was chosen from a candidate list of eight sites in 1950. The construction contractor was F.H. McGraw of <a href="/wiki/Hartford,_Connecticut" title="Hartford, Connecticut">Hartford, Connecticut</a> and the operating company was <a href="/wiki/Union_Carbide" title="Union Carbide">Union Carbide</a>. The plant was opened in 1952 as a government-owned, contractor-operated facility producing enriched uranium to fuel military reactors and for use in nuclear weapons. The mode of enrichment was the <a href="/wiki/Gaseous_diffusion" title="Gaseous diffusion">gaseous diffusion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Uranium_hexaflouride" title="Uranium hexaflouride" class="mw-redirect">uranium hexaflouride</a> to separate the lighter <a href="/wiki/Fissile" title="Fissile" class="mw-redirect">fissile</a> isotope U-235 from the heavier non-<a href="/wiki/Fissile" title="Fissile" class="mw-redirect">fissile</a> isotope U-238. The Paducah plant originally produced low-enriched uranium which was further refined at Portsmouth and the <a href="/wiki/K-25" title="K-25">K-25</a> plant at <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge,_Tennessee" title="Oak Ridge, Tennessee">Oak Ridge, Tennessee</a>. From the 1960s the Portsmouth and Paducah plants were dedicated to uranium enrichment for nuclear power plants. In 1984 the operating contract was assumed by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Marietta" title="Martin Marietta">Martin Marietta Energy Systems</a>. <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> has operated the plant since the merger of Martin Marietta with Lockheed in 1995. From 2001, all USEC production has been consolidated at Paducah.<sup id="cite_ref-usec1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usec1-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-usec3_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usec3-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>The Paducah plant had a capacity of 11.3 million <a href="/wiki/Separative_Work_Unit" title="Separative Work Unit" class="mw-redirect">separative work units</a> per year (SWU/year) in 1984. 1812 stages were located in five buildings: C-310 with 60 stages, C-331 with 400 stages, C-333 with 480 stages, C-335 with 400 stages and C-337 with 472 stages.<sup id="cite_ref-nwdb3-1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nwdb3-1-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>Before its downsizing and final cessation of uranium enrichment on May 31, 2013, the Paducah facility consumed about 3,000 <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">megawatts</a> of electricity at peak operation.<sup id="cite_ref-Paducah-end_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Paducah-end-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Power for the Paducah gaseous diffusion plant came from the <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Valley_Authority" title="Tennessee Valley Authority">Tennessee Valley Authority</a> (TVA). In 2012 the majority of the TVA grid was generated by coal fired plants, with three nuclear power plants counting for about 30 percent of TVA's energy.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>Geoffrey Sea of “Neighbors for an Ohio Valley Alternative” wrote a series of articles on USEC in the ecowatch news site.<sup id="cite_ref-tornado20120302_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tornado20120302-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Closure_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Closure-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Countdown_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Countdown-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-slow-cooker_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-slow-cooker-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Paducah-end_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Paducah-end-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-stiffed_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stiffed-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-KY_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KY-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-washout_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-washout-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Although in 1996 the National Academy of Sciences estimated that total costs of gaseous diffusion cleanup ranged from $8 billion to $46 billion,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> it was reported that USEC plant's decontamination and decommissioning (D&amp;D) fund was diverted and the surcharge was waived.<sup id="cite_ref-Closure_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Closure-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The reporter further asserted that USEC had threatened the Department of Energy with "a sudden shutoff of power at Paducah at the end of May (2013)" despite its charter "to return the facility to DOE 'in safe condition.'”<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>In May 2013, Paducah mayor Gayle Kaler said “Our priority as a community is first and foremost demanding cleanup dollars. We cannot accept a dirty shut down.”<sup id="cite_ref-slow-cooker_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-slow-cooker-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> It should be noted that the Paducah Gaseous Diffusion Plant is located outside the city of Paducah. When USEC ceased enrichment operations at its <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth_Gaseous_Diffusion_Plant" title="Portsmouth Gaseous Diffusion Plant">Portsmouth gaseous diffusion plant</a> in Piketon, Ohio in May 2001, it reportedly did not purge the diffusion cells although it had nine years time and had received the funding.<sup id="cite_ref-stiffed_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stiffed-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> In late May 2013 the reporter predicted a dirty power down at Paducah.<sup id="cite_ref-Countdown_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Countdown-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> As of late June 2013, it was reported that USEC had shut down about 60 percent of the cascade, with the remainder to be shut down over the summer.<sup id="cite_ref-KY_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KY-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>On November 17, 2013 a tornado damaged the Paducah gaseous diffusion plant.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> A spokesman for the plant operator, USEC Inc., reported that damage was limited to the exterior of one of the four enrichment production buildings, adjacent cooling towers and an electrical switch yard. The spokesman stated that there was no release of hazardous or radioactive materials.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> In the previous year, on March 2, 2012 the "Camp Creek tornado" reportedly stopped a few miles short of the Piketon, Ohio plant.<sup id="cite_ref-tornado20120302_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tornado20120302-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>Geoffrey Sea wrote in September 2013, USEC's demise will be either by creditors (by October 2014 loan repayment deadline), "regulators who find their spines", or by "repeal of the USEC Privatization Act by Congress."<sup id="cite_ref-washout_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-washout-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Employment_and_Economic_Impact">Employment and Economic Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paducah_Gaseous_Diffusion_Plant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Employment and Economic Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>USEC employed around 1100 to operate the plant. The Department of Energy employs around 600 through contractors to maintain the grounds, portions of the infrastructure, and to remediate environmental contamination at the site. The facility has had a positive economic impact on the local economy and continues to be an economic driver for the community. Elected officials work to ensure that the plant continues to operate though other methods of enriching uranium such as centrifuge are more efficient.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_http:.2F.2Fatomicinsights.com.2F2011.2F05.2Fmcconnell-asks-doe-to-keep-using-60-year-old-enrichment-plant-to-save-jobs.html"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paducah_Gaseous_Diffusion_Plant#endnote_http:.2F.2Fatomicinsights.com.2F2011.2F05.2Fmcconnell-asks-doe-to-keep-using-60-year-old-enrichment-plant-to-save-jobs.html">[1]</a></sup></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contamination">Contamination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paducah_Gaseous_Diffusion_Plant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Contamination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>Plant operations have contaminated the site over time. The primary contamination of concern is <a href="/wiki/Trichloroethylene" title="Trichloroethylene">trichloroethylene</a> (TCE), which was a commonly used degreaser at the site. TCE leaked and contaminated groundwater on and off the site. The groundwater is also contaminated with trace amounts of <a href="/wiki/Technetium" title="Technetium">technetium</a>-99, a radioactive fission product; Other contaminants include <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl" title="Polychlorinated biphenyl">polychlorinated biphenyl</a> (PCBs). Through normal operations, portions of the plant are contaminated with uranium.</p> <p>In 1988 TCE and trace amounts of technetium-99 was found in the drinking water wells of residences located near the plant site in McCracken County, Kentucky. To protect human health the Department of Energy provided city water at no cost to the affected residents, and continues to do so.</p> <p>In 1999, Secretary of Energy <a href="/wiki/Bill_Richardson" title="Bill Richardson">Bill Richardson</a> ordered a 24 hour stand down to assess the level of contamination.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> This followed reports of "radioactive black ooze seeping from the ground a quarter mile" away, three buried drums found behind a residents yard,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> and thick uranium dust at the plant.</p> <p>On April 30, 2013 a contamination event occurred following a pressure spike during unloading of UF<sub>6</sub> cylinder causes in the C-337A Feed Facility at the Paducah enrichment plant.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wise-epusec_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wise-epusec-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuits">Lawsuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paducah_Gaseous_Diffusion_Plant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lawsuits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>In the 1980s, the family of former employee Joe Harding brought a lawsuit relating to medical conditions that they believed he incurred from having worked at the Paducah plant. His widow Clara Harding eventually settled the suit for $12,000.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>In 1998 a lawsuit was brought by employees of the plant against Lockheed Martin, one of the operators of the Paducah plant, relating to falsifying of contamination reports.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> The Department of Energy later joined this suit.</p> <p>In 1999 a class action lawsuit was brought against the Paducah plant for former and current employees who believed that they had suffered significant medical expenses because of exposure to raditiation at the plant. The suit was dismissed in 2003 because a judge ruled that the plant was covered by the <a href="/wiki/Price-Anderson_Act" title="Price-Anderson Act" class="mw-redirect">Price-Anderson Act</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cleanup_status">Cleanup status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paducah_Gaseous_Diffusion_Plant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cleanup status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>The Department of Energy is using electrical resistance heating, ET-DSP (trademarked) to vaporize the TCE from the groundwater. This cleanup action began in mid-2008. Groundwater and soil remediation efforts are still being performed as of mid-2014.
