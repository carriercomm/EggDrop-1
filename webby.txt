in Ebola exposure</p><div style="clear: both;"></div></a></div></div></div><p>The nurse, Amber Joy Vinson, visited northeast Ohio from Oct. 10-13, when she then boarded a commercial flight in Cleveland to return to Dallas. Federal officials later reported that the nurse was traveling while running a low fever, a symptom of Ebola infection. She now is undergoing treatment for the illness.</p><p>Vinson and another nurse have come down with the Ebola virus after they cared for a Liberian man who flew to Dallas in September then became sick. He died Oct. 8.</p><p>The Ohio Department of Health would not release more specific information about the Hamilton County residents – including a precise number of those affected or their names. Monday's status report simply said: "Fifteen counties have seven or less contacts."</p><div id="module-position-NmoBeIP6FgE" class="story-asset oembed-asset"><div class="oembed oembed-cincinnati oembed-type-link" data-oembed-type="link" data-oembed-provider="cincinnati"><style> .oembed-asset-link { background: #fff; border-bottom: 1px solid #e1e1e1; } .oembed-link-anchor { display: block; clear: both; } .oembed-link-thumbnail{ float: left; padding: 14px; } .oembed-link-thumbnail img { max-width: 78px; max-height: 60px; display: block; } p.oembed-link-title { font-size: 75%; color: #009BFF; margin: 0 14px; padding-top: 12px; font-weight:normal; text-align: left; line-height: 120%;  } p.oembed-link-desc { font-size: 100%; color: #666; font-weight: normal; margin: 0 14px 14px 14px; font-family: 'Futura Today Light'; text-align: left; line-height: 120%; } </style><div class="oembed-asset oembed-asset-link oembed-asset-cincinnati"><a href="http://www.cincinnati.com/story/news/2014/10/15/local-health-officials-prepared-ebola/17311365/?from=global&amp;sessionKey=&amp;autologin=" class="oembed-link-anchor"><div class="oembed-link-thumbnail"><img src="http://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/9065941e142eb769bb76794c742e08d1e14ee558/r=300/http/www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/6524e6304a93a8d1ac2e6074303f4a7f84449baf/c=0-200-3856-2369&amp;r=x1803&amp;c=3200x1800/local/-/media/Cincinnati/2014/10/16/aptopixebolaohio.jpg" /></div><p class="oembed-link-title">CINCINNATI</p><p class="oembed-link-desc">If Ebola came here, what would we do?</p><div style="clear: both;"></div></a></div></div></div><p>Department spokeswoman Ashley McDonald said the names would not be released as a matter of privacy. "It becomes an issue of, it's very easy to find out who those folks are. With patient privacy, we have to be very careful about releasing information that would pinpoint the people in an area."</p><p>Fifty-six people in those 15 counties are under various levels of monitoring. Nine are under "active" watch, meaning they must take their temperatures twice daily, including once by a public health official, and they are not permitted to travel commercially. These nine had no direct contact with the Dallas nurse, but were within 3 feet of her for more than one hour.</p><p>Another 33 people are also taking their temperatures twice daily and must report the findings to public health officials. They are not permitted to travel overseas. These people were within the same enclosed space as the nurse for more than one hour, but not within three feet of her.</p><p>Fourteen people must take their temperatures twice daily but need not report to public health officials; they were in the same enclosed space as the nurse for less than one hour.</p><p>The other counties with affected Ohioans are: Belmont, Erie, Franklin, Geauga, Hardin, Lake, Lorain, Mahoning, Medina, Portage, Putnam, Stark, Tuscarawas and Wayne.</p><p>One person in Cuyahoga County and two people in Summit County are in quarantine for coming in contact with Vinson, the nurse, who was visiting in northeast Ohio between Oct. 10 and 13. Eleven more people in those two counties must take their temperatures at least twice a day -- at least once a day by a public-health official.</p><p>Local health officials placed another 83 people under less-restrictive monitoring.</p><p>Ebola is transmitted through direct contact with bodily fluid, such as blood or vomit, after a person develops symptoms. Vinson registered a low-grade temperature on her last day in Ohio before she flew home to Texas.</p><p>Melissa Ayers, another Ohio Department of Health spokeswoman, said, "It's very difficult to transmit Ebola, but we're going above and beyond, and we're taking extra precaution to make sure that we keep people safe."</p><p>Later Monday, the State Controlling Board gave the health department the OK to spent $300,000 in federal and state money to buy additional personal protective equipment for front-line health care workers. Another $500,000 was approved for cleanup and disposal of Ebola-contaminated materials.</p><p>Staff writer Chrissie Thompson contributed to this story.</p><div id="module-position-NmoBeIOhBjg"
