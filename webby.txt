<p><b>Fatal familial insomnia</b> (FFI) is a very rare <a href="/wiki/Autosome" title="Autosome">autosomal</a> <a href="/wiki/Dominance_relationship" title="Dominance relationship" class="mw-redirect">dominant inherited</a> <a href="/wiki/Prion_disease" title="Prion disease" class="mw-redirect">prion disease</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">brain</a>. It is almost always caused by a <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutation</a> to the protein <a href="/wiki/PRNP" title="PRNP">PrP<sup>C</sup></a>, but can also develop spontaneously in patients with a non-inherited mutation variant called <b>sporadic fatal insomnia</b> (sFI). FFI has no known cure and involves progressively worsening <a href="/wiki/Insomnia" title="Insomnia">insomnia</a>, which leads to hallucinations, delirium, and confusional states like that of dementia.<sup id="cite_ref-Schenkein06_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schenkein06-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The average survival span for patients diagnosed with FFI after the onset of symptoms is 18 months.<sup id="cite_ref-Schenkein06_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schenkein06-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>The mutated protein, called PrP<sup>Sc</sup>, has been found in just 40 families worldwide, affecting about 100 people; if only one parent has the gene, the offspring have a 50% risk of inheriting it and developing the disease. With onset landing around middle age it is essential that any potential patient be tested. Otherwise one would realize they had FFI only after potentially passing it on to their children. The first recorded case was an <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italian</a> man, who died in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> in the year 1765.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Family_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Family-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p></p> <div id="toc" class="toc"> <div id="toctitle"> <h2>Contents</h2> href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">9</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li> </ul> </div> <p></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Presentation">Presentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatal_familial_insomnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Presentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>The age of onset is variable, ranging from 18 to 60, with an average of 50. However the disease tends to occur prominently in later years, primarily following giving birth. The disease can be detected prior to onset by genetic testing.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Death usually occurs between 7 and 36 months from onset. The presentation of the disease varies considerably from person to person, even among patients from within the same family.</p> <p>The disease has four stages, taking 7 to 18 months to run its course:</p> <ol> <li>The person has increasing <a href="/wiki/Insomnia" title="Insomnia">insomnia</a>, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Panic_attack" title="Panic attack">panic attacks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paranoia" title="Paranoia">paranoia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phobia" title="Phobia">phobias</a>. This stage lasts for about four months.</li> <li><a href="/wiki/Hallucination" title="Hallucination">Hallucinations</a> and panic attacks become noticeable, continuing for about five months.</li> <li>Complete inability to <a href="/wiki/Sleep" title="Sleep">sleep</a> is followed by rapid loss of <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a>. This lasts for about three months.</li> <li><a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">Dementia</a>, during which the patient becomes unresponsive or mute over the course of six months. This is the final progression of the disease, after which death follows.</li> </ol> <p>Other symptoms include profuse sweating, <a href="/wiki/Miosis" title="Miosis">pinpoint pupils</a>, the sudden entrance into <a href="/wiki/Menopause" title="Menopause">menopause</a> for women and <a href="/wiki/Impotence" title="Impotence" class="mw-redirect">impotence</a> for men, neck stiffness, and <a href="/wiki/High_blood_pressure" title="High blood pressure" class="mw-redirect">elevation of blood pressure</a> and heart rate. Constipation is common as well. As the disease progresses the patient is forever stuck in a state of pre-sleep limbo. During these stages it is common for patients to repeatedly move their limbs as if dreaming.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>The first case was reported in the Netherlands of a 57 year old man of Egyptian descent.<sup id="cite_ref-Scientific_Correspondence_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scientific_Correspondence-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The man came in with symptoms of double vision and progressive memory loss, and his family also noted he had recently become disoriented, overly paranoid and confused. While he tended to fall asleep during random daily activities, he experienced vivid dreams and random muscular jerks during normal slow wave sleep.<sup id="cite_ref-Scientific_Correspondence_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scientific_Correspondence-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> After 4 months of these symptoms, he started having convulsions in the hands, trunk, and lower limbs while awake. The patient died at 58 (7 months after the onset of symptoms.) An autopsy was completed post-mortem which revealed mild <a href="/wiki/Atrophy" title="Atrophy">atrophy</a> of the frontal cortex and moderate atrophy of the <a href="/wiki/Thalamus" title="Thalamus">thalamus</a>. The atrophy of the thalamus is one of the most common signs of fatal familial insomnia.<sup id="cite_ref-Scientific_Correspondence_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scientific_Correspondence-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">Cause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatal_familial_insomnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <div class="thumb tright"> <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Location_of_PRNP-gene_in_chromosome_20.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Location_of_PRNP-gene_in_chromosome_20.png/150px-Location_of_PRNP-gene_in_chromosome_20.png" width="150" height="196" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Location_of_PRNP-gene_in_chromosome_20.png/225px-Location_of_PRNP-gene_in_chromosome_20.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d5/Location_of_PRNP-gene_in_chromosome_20.png 2x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="392" /></a> <div class="thumbcaption"> <div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Location_of_PRNP-gene_in_chromosome_20.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div> Chromosome 20</div> </div> </div> <p>Gene PRNP that provides instructions for making the <a href="/wiki/Prion_protein" title="Prion protein" class="mw-redirect">prion protein</a> PrP<sup>C</sup> is located on the short (p) arm of chromosome 20 at position p13.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Both FFI patients and those with familial <a href="/wiki/Creutzfeldt-Jakob_disease" title="Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease" class="mw-redirect">Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease</a> carry a mutation at codon 178 of the prion protein gene. FFI is invariably linked to the presence of the <a href="/wiki/Methionine" title="Methionine">methionine</a> codon at position 129 of the mutant allele, whereas fCJD is linked to the presence of the <a href="/wiki/Valine" title="Valine">valine</a> codon at that position.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> "The disease is where there is a change of amino acid at position 178 when an asparagine (N) is found instead of the normal aspartic acid (D). This has to be accompanied with a methionine at position 129."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatal_familial_insomnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <div class="thumb tright"> <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sleep_Hypnogram.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Sleep_Hypnogram.svg/220px-Sleep_Hypnogram.svg.png" width="220" height="130" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Sleep_Hypnogram.svg/330px-Sleep_Hypnogram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Sleep_Hypnogram.svg/440px-Sleep_Hypnogram.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="605" data-file-height="357" /></a> <div class="thumbcaption"> <div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sleep_Hypnogram.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div> Normal sleep. FFI–patients cannot pass stage 1.</div> </div> </div> <p>Normal sleep has different stages that together last 90 to 100 minutes:</p> <ul> <li>Non REM Stages 1 and 2: Light sleep <a href="/wiki/Non-rapid_eye_movement_sleep" title="Non-rapid eye movement sleep">NREM-sleep</a></li> <li>Non REM Stages 3 and 4: Deep <a href="/wiki/Slow_wave_sleep" title="Slow wave sleep" class="mw-redirect">slow wave sleep</a> (SWS)</li> <li><a href="/wiki/Rapid_eye_movement_sleep" title="Rapid eye movement sleep">REM-sleep</a> when dreams occur</li> </ul> <p>FFI patients cannot go past stage 1 and thus their brains are not getting the rest they need to revive, as most reviving and repairing processes of the body are believed to happen during these deeper sleep stages.<sup id="cite_ref-DyingWithout_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DyingWithout-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> For example, psychiatrist <a href="/wiki/Ian_Oswald" title="Ian Oswald">Ian Oswald</a> observed that during slow wave sleep (SWS) the <a href="/wiki/Pituitary_gland" title="Pituitary gland">pituitary gland</a> increases its secretion of growth hormones. This discovery led Oswald to conclude that SWS restores the wear and tear bodily tissues gain throughout the day.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Since a patient with FFI would be unable to reach SWS, and therefore the process of restoration during sleep, their body would become more worn each passing day.</p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatal_familial_insomnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>In late 1983, Italian <a href="/wiki/Neurologist" title="Neurologist" class="mw-redirect">neurologist</a>/sleep expert Dr. Ignazio Roiter received a patient at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bologna" title="University of Bologna">University of Bologna</a> hospital's sleep institute. The man, known only as Silvano, decided in a rare moment of consciousness to be recorded for future studies and to donate his brain for research in hopes of finding a cure for future victims. As of 2014, no cure or treatment has yet been found for FFI. <a href="/wiki/Gene_therapy" title="Gene therapy">Gene therapy</a> has been thus far unsuccessful. While it is not currently possible to reverse the underlying illness, there is some evidence that treatments that focus solely upon the symptoms may improve <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid17406189_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17406189-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>It has been proven that <a href="/wiki/Sleeping_pill" title="Sleeping pill" class="mw-redirect">sleeping pills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barbiturate" title="Barbiturate">barbiturates</a> are unhelpful; on the contrary, in 74% of cases they have been shown to worsen the clinical manifestations and hasten the course of the disease.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>One of the most notable cases is that of Michael (Michel A.) Corke, a music teacher from <a href="/wiki/New_Lenox" title="New Lenox" class="mw-redirect">New Lenox</a>, Illinois (born in <a href="/wiki/Watseka" title="Watseka">Watseka</a>, Illinois). He began to have trouble sleeping before his 40th birthday in 1991; following these first signs of insomnia, his health and state of mind quickly deteriorated as his condition worsened. Eventually, sleep became completely unattainable, and he was soon admitted to <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago_Hospital" title="University of Chicago Hospital" class="mw-redirect">University of Chicago Hospital</a> with a misdiagnosis of <a href="/wiki/Clinical_depression" title="Clinical depression" class="mw-redirect">clinical depression</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Multiple_sclerosis" title="Multiple sclerosis">multiple sclerosis</a>. Medical professionals, (Dr Raymond Roos and Dr Anthony Reder) at first unsure of the nature of his illness, initially diagnosed <a href="/wiki/Multiple_sclerosis" title="Multiple sclerosis">multiple sclerosis</a>; in a bid to provide temporary relief in the later stages of the disease, physicians induced a <a href="/wiki/Coma" title="Coma">coma</a> with the use of sedatives, to no avail as his brain still failed to shut down completely. Corke died in 1993, a month after his 42nd birthday, by which time he had been completely sleep-deprived for six months.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>One patient was able to exceed the average survival time by nearly one year with various strategies, including vitamin therapy and <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">meditation</a>, using different stimulants and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcoleptics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Narcoleptics (page does not exist)">narcoleptics</a> and even complete <a href="/wiki/Sensory_deprivation" title="Sensory deprivation">sensory deprivation</a> in an attempt to induce sleep at night and increase alertness during the day. He managed to write a book and drive hundreds of miles in this time but nonetheless, over the course of his trials, the patient succumbed to the classic four-stage progression of the illness.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid17406189_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17406189-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DyingWithout_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DyingWithout-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>In the late 2000s, a mouse model was made for FFI. These mice expressed a humanized version of the PrP protein that also contains the D178N FFI mutation.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> These mice appear to have progressively fewer and shorter periods of uninterrupted sleep, damage in the <a href="/wiki/Thalamus" title="Thalamus">thalamus</a>, and early deaths, similar to humans with FFI.</p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">Epidemiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatal_familial_insomnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Epidemiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>There are 25 reported families in the world that carry the gene for FFI: 5 Italian, 2 French, 4 American, 1 Japanese, 2 Australian, 8 German, 1 Austrian, and 2 British.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> There are other prion diseases that are similar to FFI and could be related but are missing the D178N gene mutation which puts them out of this specific gene.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2011 another family was added to the list when researchers found the first man in the Netherlands with FFI. While he had lived in the Netherlands for 19 years, he was of Egyptian descent.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>Only nine cases of sporadic fatal insomnia have ever been diagnosed as of July 2005<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fatal_familial_insomnia&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> In sFI there is no mutation in <i>PRNP</i>-prion gene in D178N, but all have <a href="/wiki/Methionine" title="Methionine">methionine</a> <a href="/wiki/Homozygosity" title="Homozygosity" class="mw-redirect">homozygosity</a> at <a href="/wiki/Codon" title="Codon" class="mw-redirect">codon</a> 129. <sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_conditions">Related conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatal_familial_insomnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Related conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>There are other diseases involving the <a href="/wiki/PrP" title="PrP" class="mw-redirect">mammalian prion protein</a>. Some are transmissible (<a href="/wiki/Transmissible_spongiform_encephalopathy" title="Transmissible spongiform encephalopathy">TSEs</a>) such as <a href="/wiki/Kuru_(disease)" title="Kuru (disease)">kuru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bovine_spongiform_encephalopathy" title="Bovine spongiform encephalopathy">bovine spongiform encephalopathy</a> (BSE, also known as "mad cow disease") in cows, and <a href="/wiki/Chronic_wasting_disease" title="Chronic wasting disease">chronic wasting disease</a> in American <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elk" title="Elk">American elk</a> in some areas of the United States and Canada, as well as <a href="/wiki/Creutzfeldt-Jakob_disease" title="Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease" class="mw-redirect">Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease</a> (CJD). Until recently prion diseases were only thought to be transmissible via direct contact with infected tissue, such as from eating infected tissue, transfusion or transplantation; new research now suggests that prion diseases can be transmitted via aerosols, but that the general public is not at risk of airborne infection.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatal_familial_insomnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>The disease received a mention in the <i><a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order:_Special_Victims_Unit" title="Law &amp; Order: Special Victims Unit">Law &amp; Order: Special Victims Unit</a></i> episode "<a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order:_Special_Victims_Unit_(season_12)" title="Law &amp; Order: Special Victims Unit (season 12)">Bombshell</a>", where a previously successful Italian-American immigrant lost two fingertips and his job in an industrial accident and ended up homeless after the onset of symptoms.</p> <p>Another mention of the disease can be found in the <i><a href="/wiki/Inspector_Lewis" title="Inspector Lewis">Inspector Lewis</a></i> episode "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Inspector_Lewis_(season_4)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Inspector Lewis (season 4) (page does not exist)">Falling Darkness</a>".</p> <p>In 2006, the Discovery Channel released <i>My Shocking Story: Dying to Sleep,</i> a documentary about FFI and the lives of those affected. One of the cases observed is that of two female twins. Their mother had died from FFI and neither woman knew if they also had the disease.</p> <p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Ghost_Whisperer" title="Ghost Whisperer">Ghost Whisperer</a></i> episode "<a href="/wiki/Ghost_Whisperer_(season_5)" title="Ghost Whisperer (season 5)">Living Nightmare</a>" focuses on a man whose family has the disease.</p> <p>The short story <i>Pathways</i> by <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Kress" title="Nancy Kress">Nancy Kress</a> (2014) centers around the technological development of using deep-brain stimulation with special algae to treat fatal familial insomnia.</p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatal_familial_insomnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatal_familial_insomnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;"> <ol class="references"> <li id="cite_note-Schenkein06-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-Schenkein06_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-Schenkein06_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"><span class="citation journal">Schenkein J, Montagna P (2006). <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.medscape.com/viewarticle/542963">"Self management of fatal familial insomnia.
