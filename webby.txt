astonishing car, with its butterfly-wing doors, sensuous curves and lightweight alloy and composite construction, combines a modified Mini's engine with an electric motor to help it put out just 49 grams of CO2 per kilometre and to travel up to 37km on electric power alone, if you wish it to.   </p>    <p>      The fact that it can reach 100kmh in 4.4 seconds is not surprising, if you judge it by the way it looks, but the first thing to realise about BMW's i8 is that it is not an all-out track-day supercar, like the $1 million-plus ultra- hybrids from Porsche, Ferrari and McLaren.   </p>    <p>      True, it's darn near as quick and sticks to the road as if on Velcro, but for day-to-day driving, this BMW i-Division bullet can be as much of a pussycat as you want it to be, coping gently and easily with a daily commute with as little fuss as your favourite hatchback. That it can then show a clean set of composite curves to serious (and thirsty) performance machinery is nice to know, but not its raison d'etre.   </p>    <p>      The car's amazing sports-coupe 2+2 looks attract the gaze of passers-by like spectators' eyes to the ball in a tennis rally, possibly because they expect it to be the supercar it isn't, but also because the scrolling curves of its carbon-fibre construction, especially over the rear wheel arches and the muscular road-hugging stance is a unique and compelling sight.   </p>    <p>      People don't walk straight up to the i8 and cup their brows as they try to stare inside.   </p>    <p>      Instead, they stop about 6 or 7 metres short of the car to take in the shape and the way the light works over it as the wind moves the clouds overhead.   </p>    <p>      The wind resistance of the car scores a 0.26 drag coefficient, which confirms that the shape must be right, but any person with an eye for form and art will know this instinctively and the way they smile, tilt their heads and pass their hands over my car's &quot;crystal white pearl&quot; paint is with respect and appreciation.   </p>    <p>      So, if it's not an all-out supercar, what exactly is a BMW i8? BMW says its i-Division, just like its M-Division, has a special purpose, representing, in the Munich company's words &quot;visionary electric vehicles and mobility services, inspiring design and a new understanding of &quot;premium&quot; that is strongly defined by sustainability&quot;.   </p>    <p>      This explains the need for the car to be a day-to-day prospect, as well as amazingly, relaxingly fast. It's designed to be much more versatile than an ordinary supercar, with fewer compromises than the beasts mentioned above, and though it has its own unique complexities, the truly elegant sufficiencies of its methods of construction and its powertrain are as remarkable as they are relevant to the immediate future of motoring.   </p>    <p>      BMW already has pure electric vehicles on the market, but it sees plug-in hybrids like the i8 and range-extender cars like the i3 as its i-Division's stock-in-trade until things like hydrogen fuel cells can provide those extensions.   </p>    <p>      Under those delicious curves, in order to keep weight down, BMW gave the i8 a wide aluminium spine chassis on top of which is a carbon-fibre reinforced plastic (CFRP) passenger cell. The curves themselves are formed from the aluminium and composite body panels attached to the cell.   </p>      <!--start of components/ad/story_rectangle-->  <!--start of components/ad/standard-->    <div class="ad_story_island hidden_ad" style="" id="adSTORYBODY">      <!--start of components/ad/feedback_link-->  <span class="ad_leftcol_feedback" id="feedback_STORYBODY">   <a class="adfeedback" target="_blank" href="/about-stuff/advertising-feedback/?pos=STORYBODY&adsize=300x250&area=s.stuff">    Ad Feedback</a> </span> <!--end of components/ad/feedback_link-->    </div>    <!--start of components/ad/push-->  <script language="javascript"> <!--  PushDartJSAd('STORYBODY', null, 300, 250, '300x250');  //--> </script> <!--end of components/ad/push-->  <!--end of components/ad/standard-->  <!--end of components/ad/story_rectangle-->    <p>      The i8's drivetrain, electrically assisted steering and wishbone front/multilink rear suspension are mounted to the chassis from subframes. The main power comes from a warmed-over three- cylinder Mini Cooper engine, a 170kW/320Nm, 1.5-litre unit which sits across the mid-rear of the car and drives the rear wheels through a six-speed automatic transmission.   </p>    <p>      Under the rear seats are the fuel tank and a 100-kilogram, 7.1-kilowatt/hour lithium-ion battery pack and between the front wheels is a 96kW/250Nm AC electric motor driving the front wheels through a two-speed transmission. To prevent the instant torque delivery of the electric power unit from dominating the petrol-powered rear engine, the triple is assisted by a 9.6kW/100Nm starter- generator which cleverly helps spool up the twin-scroll turbocharger.   </p>    <p>      The i-Division's obsession with saving weight is taken further by the use of foam plastics for climate-control ducting, while aluminum wiring replaces the heavier conventional copper variety, and aluminum nuts, screws and bolts are used instead of steel. Even the glazing is thinner and lighter and it all results in a car weighing 1490kg, similar to a family-sized four-cylinder sedan.   </p>    <p>      Butterfly doors add to the visual appeal and, once you get the entry technique right, which is to sit on the sill and then bring your legs in while sliding your soft bits into the front seats, it's not an inelegant way of getting into a car, though you'll have to practise if you regularly wear a skirt or dress.   </p>    <p>      While the car is labelled a two- plus-two, it's really nothing of the sort unless your rear passengers are children or contortionists. Far better to use the rear cabin for luggage, as the rear boot will take not much more than a work bag or computer satchel, though BMW says fitted Louis Vuitton luggage is available.   </p>    <p>      Unlike other mid-engined cars, the i8 does not have extra load space up front, as that's where the electric motor is placed.   </p>    <p>      However, the cabin is more than comfortable for the two main users, with slim, well-shaped seats that afford a huge amount of fore and aft adjustment for all sorts of shapes and sizes and, while the driving position is a fairly typical lie-back type, it manages to afford a good view out, and the not-so- visible rearward quarters are well- served by the car's camera and screen. The interior fit and finish is exemplary, but simple materials and textures avoid the brashness of some ultra modern cars' execution, like the Tesla S, while also avoiding the stark austerity of the aforementioned superhybrids. The effect is subtle and pleasant. It's a nice place to be.   </p>    <p>      The i8 offers several driving modes and it will take some time to work out which blend of the two drive systems you prefer for day- to-day running.   </p>    <p>      Pure electricity or the E-setting can be used for stealthy driving though quiet neighbourhoods up to a speed of 125kmh and a distance of 37km. It's brisk and silent.   </p>    <p>      A mashed throttle, or the Sport setting brings in both power sources which can be employed to their fullest outputs and this is when the i8's triple-cylinder music starts to make itself heard and its power through all fours records that mid-four-second 0-100kmh time.   </p>    <p>      Comfort is the car's default setting and thus selected the car's onboard brain will shuffle between its power sources to suit, drawing charge down to about 30 per cent before engaging the petrol engine to deliver more charge to the electric power system.   </p>    <p>      Left to these devices the car should be able to maintain such a charge for some time.   </p>    <p>      Using the paddle shifters for the six-speed main transmission, the i8 can be driven in much the same way other as BMWs with similar performance, models such as the M3 and M4, for instance.   </p>    <p>      But, with the lowest centre of gravity of any BMW in the company's history, it's able to use its full potential with amazing alacrity and almost unstickable gusto. The steering responds well to the driver and delivers good feedback and, like the whole car, it works in a linear and predictable fashion.   </p>    <p>      So undemonstrative is this supercar, that it really doesn't seem as quick as it is and, despite having a low-slung, ground- hugging chassis, it demonstrates uncannily comfortable ride quality. You just don't expect a vehicle with a near-absence of understeer and an amazingly biddable nature like this to cross holes and bumps with such lack of fuss.   </p>    <p>      It imbues great confidence, and calm, unlike the tramlining twitchiness of many mid and rear engined cars of similar performance. It would be an easy choice for a length-of-New Zealand trip - as long as you have a two- hour stop here and there to jolt an 80 per cent charge into the electrical bits.   </p>    <p>      That's not to say the chassis is absolutely perfect, because it isn't.   </p>    <p>      While I was still getting used to the car, I was understandably reticent and, on backing off and reapplying that prodigious power, there was the slightest change of front-rear balance.   </p>    <p>      It wasn't like conventional under or oversteer, but more a sense that the rear driven part of the car was competing with rather than conspiring with the front- driven electric part.   </p>    <p>      I'm not sure if I could manage the maximum posted economy figures for the car of 2.1 litres per 100km; it's too tempting a powertrain to be left to Comfort and E mode for too long.   </p>    <p>      When I did find myself driving at easy pace, every driver out there on Auckland's motorway seemed to want to tailgate the car and coax its driver into doing something more in line with what the car's body shape says it should be doing.   </p>    <p>      One yellow car got so close that my navigator couldn't even read the number plate.
