What&#39;s the worst thing that can happen if you misuse a pressure cooker in an ordinary kitchen?</p> <p id="attribute">—Delphine Lourtau</p> <p>The <em>worst</em> thing?</p> <img class="illustration" title="why are you humming ice ice baby?" src="/imgs/a/40/pressure_cooker_hmm.png"> <p>Pressure cookers are dangerous.</p> <p>They can explode, in a sense, but not as violently as you might fear (or hope). The pressure inside a consumer cooker doesn’t go above about two atmospheres—about the pressure <a href="http://hypertextbook.com/facts/2000/SeemaMeraj.shtml">inside a can of soda</a>. Those levels can be dangerous, but they’re generally not high enough to cause the metal to violently rupture.</p> <p>So what makes a pressure cooker dangerous?</p> <p>Imagine a world where Pepsi is scalding hot. Now imagine that someone shakes up a can of Pepsi and sets it in front of you.</p> <img class="illustration" title="the first thing i’d do in this world is try the diet coke and mentos thing" src="/imgs/a/40/pressure_cooker_pepsi.png"> <p>That’s the real threat from a pressure cooker: If the seal fails (or the lid is opened too early), it can <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/12670995@N02/1888382766/">spray scalding stew in all directions</a>.</p> <p>But it’s not really an <em>explosion</em>.</p> <p>The blast couldn’t even fling the lid very far. If you mounted a rifle-style barrel on a pressure cooker, even in ideal circumstances it wouldn’t be able to fire the lid much faster than you could throw it. Any potato cannon (especially <a href="http://www.spudfiles.com/forums/mk-2-rotary-barrel-semi-automatic-combustion-t15766.html">this one</a>) could do better.</p> <p>Of course, the question wasn’t about whether a pressure cooker is likely to explode. It was about what the worst thing that could happen was.</p> <p>If you disable the safety valve, there are plenty of ways to produce much more dangerous pressures. You could completely fill it with water and heat it, <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YbaiCdX1XWc">fill it with Drano and aluminum foil</a>, or just pump in air from a compressor.</p> <p>The result would depend on your pressure cooker. Chances are it would start to leak. If it didn’t, and it somehow stayed together up a few hundred atmospheres (pressures typical of scuba tank), when it finally <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tyINNUaXa8Q">ruptured</a> it could easily kill you.</p> <img class="illustration" title="frankly, we&#39;re surprised she survived this long" src="/imgs/a/40/pressure_cooker_grave.png"> <p>Even so, that’s far from the <em>worst</em> thing you could do with a pressure cooker.</p> <p>Frankly, there are so many options it would be impossible to survey them all. But for my money, one of the most horrifying things you could do is this:</p> <p><em>(Note: <strong>Never</strong> try this, for reasons which will become obvious in a moment.)</em></p> <p>Fill the cooker with oxygen up to 5 PSI, then pump in fluorine until it starts escaping through the safety valve. Put the vessel over an open flame until it reaches 700°C (That’s °C, not °F. Yes, this will probably set off the smoke alarm.) Now, pump the hot gas over a liquid-oxygen-cooled stainless steel surface.</p> <p>The procedure here is a little tricky, but if you do things <a href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022113900803413">right</a>, the gas will condense into <strong>dioxygen difluoride</strong> (O2F2).</p> <p>And that stuff is <em>awful</em>.</p> <p>Ray Bradbury <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fahrenheit_451">taught us</a> that paper burns when exposed to oxygen at temperatures above 451°F. Dioxygen difluoride is so volatile that it makes almost any organic substance ignite and explode at any temperature hotter than 300°F <em>below zero.</em> It can literally make <em><strong>ice</strong></em> catch fire.</p> <p><a href="http://pipeline.corante.com/archives/2010/02/23/things_i_wont_work_with_dioxygen_difluoride.php">In an article about O</a><a href="http://pipeline.corante.com/archives/2010/02/23/things_i_wont_work_with_dioxygen_difluoride.php">2</a><a href="http://pipeline.corante.com/archives/2010/02/23/things_i_wont_work_with_dioxygen_difluoride.php">F</a><a href="http://pipeline.corante.com/archives/2010/02/23/things_i_wont_work_with_dioxygen_difluoride.php">2</a>, Chemistry blogger Derek Lowe (of the excellent <em>In The Pipeline</em>) used phrases like “violently hideous”, “deeply alarming”, and “chemicals that I never hope to encounter”. <a href="http://www.lateralscience.co.uk/Fluorine/Fluorine.html">Another article</a> refers to fluorine as “the gas of Lucifer”, and lists chemists who were poisoned or blown up while attempting to work with it.</p> <p>If your house is heated by natural gas, and it happens to contain hydrogen sulfide, you could pipe some of it into your container of O2F2. In addition to a massive explosion, this will <em>also</em> produce a cloud of hydrogen fluoride gas. Hydrogen fluoride can dissolve human tissue on contact, starting with your lungs and corneas.</p> <p>As Lowe points out, the chemistry of this kind of reaction (O2F2 and sulfides) is largely unexplored.</p> <p>Which gives us an answer to our question.
