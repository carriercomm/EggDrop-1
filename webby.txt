id="article-guts">   <div itemprop="articleBody" class="article-content clearfix">      <figure class="intro-image image center full-width" style="width:640px">       <img src="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/seeingi-640x639.png" width="640" height="639">        <figcaption class="caption">    <div class="caption-text">Artist Mark Farid will stay in a getup like this for four weeks straight, if everything goes to plan.</div>      </figcaption>   </figure>  <!-- cache hit 147:single/related:7749404bcf0ade1a08de85e705e50dba --><!-- empty --> <p>When we've tried out virtual reality headsets like the Oculus Rift in the Ars Orbiting HQ, we've never lasted more than a few hours without at least a short break into actual reality. That's partly because the current development kits form a tight, hot, sweaty seal with the front of your face and partly because the current resolution on the units has a tendency <a href="http://arstechnica.com/staff/2014/10/multi-hour-oculus-rift-sessions-are-doing-weird-things-to-my-eyes/">to cause some funny visual effects after a while</a>. For now, connecting your eyes and ears to virtual sights and sounds disconnected from the rest of your body can be a little overwhelming in such large doses.</p> <p>So we were a little impressed and a little concerned when we heard about <a href="http://www.seeing-i.co.uk/">Seeing I</a>, an art project where London-based performance artist Mark Farid pledges to wear a VR headset and headphones for 28 days straight. During that time, his sight and sound will be replaced by those of a stranger, simply called "The Other," who will be recording his everyday life through a binaural microphone and 180 degree stereo camera setup attached to his glasses (applications to be The Other <a href="http://www.seeing-i.co.uk/other/">are open</a>, if the idea of having your entire life virtually broadcast to another person for a month appeals to you)</p> <p><a href="https://www.kickstarter.com/projects/1905266932/seeing-i-28-days-living-only-through-virtual-reali">The Kickstarter page</a> for the Seeing I project is full of heady, art-poseur musings about how technology is taking over our lives and removing the link between our "real" and "virtual" selves ("As we've never been taught how to be alone, we only know how to be lonely" the Kickstarter video seriously intones). Frankly, we're more fascinated with the practicalities of the project.</p> <p>According to <a href="https://www.kickstarter.com/projects/1905266932/seeing-i-28-days-living-only-through-virtual-reali">the Kickstarter</a>, which seeks to raise £150,000 ($235072.50) for the project, "Mark will eat, shower, and go to the toilet when the Other does" and consume the same food and drinks as The Other as well. (Mark will experience the life of "The Other" on a six-day delay, to allow a small staff to facilitate all of this). The project left unsaid whether Mark will also have sex at the same time as The Other (who is required to be a heterosexual male with a committed partner), but <a href="http://www.vice.com/read/mark-farid-seeing-i-live-through-an-avatar-122">in an interview with Vice</a>, Mark said he doubts he'll give in to the urge to masturbate constantly during his sensory "deprivation."</p> <p>Mark will also have one hour a day (just after "The Other" goes to sleep) to communicate with a psychologist through his headphones. This process will "act... as verbal diary, but will also allow the psychologist to access his mental health." Other than that, "Mark will have no actual interact [sic] with any human." The whole thing will be confined to a small, publicly viewable space where lookie-loos can watch Mark alongside a projection of The Other's experiences, and it will all be filmed for the inevitable documentary.</p> <p>While the final project isn't planned to go forward until fall of 2015, Mark and his team have <a href="http://player.vimeo.com/video/107582497">already been through a 24-hour test run</a>, during which Mark blithely endured The Other making bland small talk, eating greasy fish and chips, and shopping for pants. Speaking to Vice, Mark said the only time he really felt like he was losing his sense of self was when The Other was playing <i>Grand Theft Auto</i>, which caused Mark to unconsciously twitch his hands as if he were holding a controller.</p> <p>We're a little skeptical that the project's central question—"Will Mark start to believe this new life as his own?"—will be answered with anything other than a qualified "No." Despite the VR setup and the attempts to get Mark to mimic the events of The Other's life, he will still have access to information from his other senses that will probably make it hard to fully accept his life has been taken over (and which might cause <a href="http://arstechnica.com/gaming/2014/08/developer-cites-motion-sickness-in-delaying-oculus-rift-support/">more than a little motion sickness</a>, we'd wager).</p> <p>Then again, 28 days is a long time to not be able to see or hear for yourself or to not talk to anyone but your psychologist. There's a decent chance he won't be able to make it, and the Kickstarter notes the project could easily be cut short for medical or psychological reasons.</p> <p>In any case, while Mark's reaction might tell us something about the human condition, we're not sure how relevant it will be to thinking about how VR could actually be integrated into our technological lives in the near future. For all the fear-mongering about how "isolating" living in VR can be, the most popular applications will probably be the ones that let us experience virtual spaces with others, with the ability to see their avatars, have a conversation, and, yes, take a break to the real world when necessary. The days when we can all jack into a version of <em>The Matrix</em> and leave the pains and annoyances of the real world behind for good is still a long way off.</p>   </div>     <div class="article-expander">       <p><a href="http://arstechnica.com/gaming/2014/11/artist-wants-to-walk-in-anothers-mans-virtual-reality-shoes-for-a-month/">Expand full story</a></p>     </div>        </section>       <div id="article-footer-wrap">   <section id="comments-area">      <section id="promoted-comments">     <h2 class="subheading"><span class="full">Promoted Comments</span></h2>     <ul>       <li class="comment" data-post-score="5" data-post-negative="0" data-post-positive="5" data-post-id="27995191" data-poster-id="333088">   <header class="byline">          <span class="author" data-post-author="shav"><a href="http://arstechnica.com/civis/memberlist.php?mode=viewprofile&amp;u=333088">shav</a></span><span class="rank">Ars Centurion</span>       <aside class="icons">                                      <a href="http://arstechnica.com/gaming/2014/11/artist-wants-to-walk-in-anothers-mans-virtual-reality-shoes-for-a-month/?comments=1&amp;post=27995191#comment-27995191" class="timeago datetime">jump to post</a>                  <span class="hidden-icon-start"></span>                                                    </aside>     </header>     <div class="body">       What happens if The Other gets arrested or beaten up in a bar?
