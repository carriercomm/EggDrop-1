house that led to the naming of the effect.</div> </div> </div> <p>The <b>Streisand effect</b> is the phenomenon whereby an attempt to hide, remove, or censor a piece of information has the <a href="/wiki/Unintended_consequences" title="Unintended consequences">unintended consequence</a> of publicizing the information more widely, usually facilitated by the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>.</p> <p>It is named after American entertainer <a href="/wiki/Barbra_Streisand" title="Barbra Streisand">Barbra Streisand</a>, whose 2003 attempt to suppress photographs of her residence in <a href="/wiki/Malibu,_California" title="Malibu, California">Malibu, California</a> inadvertently generated further publicity of it. Similar attempts have been made, for example, in <a href="/wiki/Cease_and_desist" title="Cease and desist">cease-and-desist letters</a> to suppress <a href="/wiki/Illegal_number" title="Illegal number">numbers</a>, files, and websites. Instead of being suppressed, the information receives extensive publicity and media extensions such as videos and spoof songs, often being widely <a href="/wiki/Web_mirror" title="Web mirror" class="mw-redirect">mirrored</a> across the Internet or distributed on <a href="/wiki/File_sharing" title="File sharing">file-sharing networks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-London_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-London-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p></p> <div id="toc" class="toc"> <div id="toctitle"> <h2>Contents</h2> </div> <ul> <li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Origin_of_the_term"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Origin of the class="toctext">External links</span></a></li> </ul> </div> <p></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_the_term">Origin of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Streisand_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p><a href="/wiki/Mike_Masnick" title="Mike Masnick">Mike Masnick</a> of <a href="/wiki/Techdirt" title="Techdirt">Techdirt</a> coined the term after Streisand unsuccessfully sued photographer Kenneth Adelman and Pictopia.com for violation of privacy.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The US$50 million lawsuit endeavored to remove an aerial photograph of Streisand's mansion from the publicly available collection of 12,000 <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> coastline photographs.<sup id="cite_ref-London_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-London-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-soctech_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soctech-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-techdirt_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-techdirt-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Adelman photographed the beachfront property to document <a href="/wiki/Coastal_erosion" title="Coastal erosion">coastal erosion</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/California_Coastal_Records_Project" title="California Coastal Records Project">California Coastal Records Project</a>, which was intended to influence government policymakers.<sup id="cite_ref-smoking_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smoking-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Before Streisand filed her lawsuit, "Image 3850" had been downloaded from Adelman's website only six times; two of those downloads were by Streisand's attorneys.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> As a result of the case, public knowledge of the picture increased substantially; more than 420,000 people visited the site over the following month.<sup id="cite_ref-rogers_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rogers-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Selected_examples">Selected examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Streisand_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Selected examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <ul> <li>In April 2007, an attempt at blocking an <a href="/wiki/AACS_encryption_key_controversy" title="AACS encryption key controversy">Advanced Access Content System (AACS) key</a> from being disseminated on <a href="/wiki/Digg" title="Digg">Digg</a> caused an uproar when cease-and-desist letters demanded the code be removed from several high-profile websites. This led to the key's proliferation across other sites and chat rooms in various formats, with one commentator describing it as having become "the most famous number on the Internet".<sup id="cite_ref-ny_most_num_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ny_most_num-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Within a month, the key had been reprinted on over 280,000 pages, had been printed on T-shirts and tattoos, and had appeared on <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> in a song played over 45,000 times.<sup id="cite_ref-greenberg_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenberg-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></li> <li>In November 2007, Tunisia blocked access to YouTube and <a href="/wiki/Dailymotion" title="Dailymotion">Dailymotion</a> after material was posted of <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisian</a> political prisoners. Activists and their supporters then started to link the location of then-President <a href="/wiki/Zine_el-Abidine_Ben_Ali" title="Zine el-Abidine Ben Ali" class="mw-redirect">Zine el-Abidine Ben Ali</a>'s palace on <a href="/wiki/Google_Earth" title="Google Earth">Google Earth</a> to videos about civil liberties in general. <i>The Economist</i> said this "turned a low-key human-rights story into a fashionable global campaign".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></li> <li>In January 2008, The <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scientology" title="Church of Scientology">Church of Scientology</a>'s unsuccessful attempts to get Internet websites to delete a video of <a href="/wiki/Tom_Cruise" title="Tom Cruise">Tom Cruise</a> speaking about <a href="/wiki/Scientology" title="Scientology">Scientology</a> resulted in the creation of <a href="/wiki/Project_Chanology" title="Project Chanology">Project Chanology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_streisand_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian_streisand-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></li> <li>On December 5, 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Watch_Foundation" title="Internet Watch Foundation">Internet Watch Foundation</a> (IWF) <a href="/wiki/Internet_Watch_Foundation_and_Wikipedia" title="Internet Watch Foundation and Wikipedia">added</a> the <a href="/wiki/English_Wikipedia" title="English Wikipedia">English Wikipedia</a> article about the 1976 <a href="/wiki/Scorpions_(band)" title="Scorpions (band)">Scorpions</a> album <i><a href="/wiki/Virgin_Killer" title="Virgin Killer">Virgin Killer</a></i> to a child pornography blacklist, considering the album's cover art "a potentially illegal indecent image of a child under the age of 18".<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_streisand_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian_streisand-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The article quickly became one of the most popular pages on the site,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> and the publicity surrounding the censorship resulted in the image being spread across other sites.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The IWF was later reported on the <a href="/wiki/BBC_News" title="BBC News">BBC News</a> website to have said "IWF's overriding objective is to minimise the availability of indecent images of children on the internet, however, on this occasion our efforts have had the opposite effect".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> This effect was also noted by the IWF in its statement about the removal of the URL from the blacklist.<sup id="cite_ref-living_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-living-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></li> <li>Other examples of attempts to remove content from Wikipedia resulting in a wider viewership of the content include the French intelligence agency <a href="/wiki/DCRI" title="DCRI" class="mw-redirect">DCRI</a>'s deletion of the French language Wikipedia article about the <a href="/wiki/Military_radio_station_of_Pierre-sur-Haute" title="Military radio station of Pierre-sur-Haute" class="mw-redirect">Military radio station of Pierre-sur-Haute</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> resulting in the article becoming the most viewed page on the <a href="/wiki/French_Wikipedia" title="French Wikipedia">French Wikipedia</a> as of April 6, 2013,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> and a 2013 libel suit by <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Katsanevas" title="Theodore Katsanevas">Theodore Katsanevas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-katsanevas_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-katsanevas-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></li> <li>In September 2009, multi-national oil company <a href="/wiki/Trafigura" title="Trafigura">Trafigura</a> obtained a <a href="/wiki/Super_injunction" title="Super injunction" class="mw-redirect">super-injunction</a> to prevent <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> newspaper from reporting on an internal Trafigura investigation into the <a href="/wiki/2006_C%C3%B4te_d%27Ivoire_toxic_waste_dump" title="2006 Côte d'Ivoire toxic waste dump" class="mw-redirect">2006 Côte d'Ivoire toxic waste dump</a> scandal, and also from reporting on even the existence of the injunction. Using <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_privilege" title="Parliamentary privilege">parliamentary privilege</a>, Labour MP <a href="/wiki/Paul_Farrelly" title="Paul Farrelly">Paul Farrelly</a> referred to the super-injunction in a parliamentary question, and on October 12, 2009, <i>The Guardian</i> reported that it had been gagged from reporting on the parliamentary question, in violation of the <a href="/wiki/Bill_of_Rights_1689" title="Bill of Rights 1689">1688 Bill of Rights</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_gagged_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_gagged-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_-_Bill_of_rights_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_-_Bill_of_rights-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Blogger Richard Wilson correctly identified the blocked question as referring to the Trafigura waste dump scandal, after which <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spectator" title="The Spectator">The Spectator</a></i> suggested the same. Not long after, Trafigura began trending on <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a>, helped along by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Fry" title="Stephen Fry">Stephen Fry</a>'s retweeting the story to his followers.<sup id="cite_ref-Stephen_Fry_spreads_news_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stephen_Fry_spreads_news-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Twitter users soon tracked down all details of the case, and by October 16, the super-injunction had been lifted and the report published.<sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph_reports_on_Trafigura_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph_reports_on_Trafigura-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></li> <li>In May 2011, <a href="/wiki/Premier_League" title="Premier League">Premier League</a> footballer <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Giggs" title="Ryan Giggs">Ryan Giggs</a> sued Twitter after a user revealed that he was the subject of an anonymous <a href="/wiki/Gag_order" title="Gag order">privacy injunction</a> (informally referred to as a "super-injunction"<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup>) that prevented the publication of details regarding an alleged affair with model and former <i><a href="/wiki/Big_Brother_(UK)" title="Big Brother (UK)">Big Brother</a></i> contestant <a href="/wiki/Imogen_Thomas" title="Imogen Thomas">Imogen Thomas</a>.
