rel="nofollow" target="_blank" class="npIconLeft npIconStumbleUpon">StumbleUpon</a></li>  </ul> </div> <h1 class="npStorySEOTitle">What does $26,000 Scotch taste like? Some Glenfiddich 50 Year Old tasting notes</h1><div class="npBlock npPostContent" itemprop="articleBody"><div itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/BlogPosting"><p>You may have read this week about what Toronto Police have alleged was <a href="http://news.nationalpost.com/2013/04/23/police-hunt-for-man-who-lefthout-paying-for-his-26000-bottle-of-scotch/" target="_blank">a remarkably audacious theft of a $26,000 bottle of 50-year-old Scotch</a> from Ontario&#8217;s flagship liquor store.</p> <p>Terrific choice, I say. I&#8217;ve tried Glenfiddich 50 Year Old, and it&#8217;s sublime.</p> <p>Many readers will be wondering what could possibly make a whisky worth $26,000. Rarity, first of all: Glenfiddich releases a mere 50 bottles of the 50-year-old annually. As well, old booze is expensive to make. Imagine the cost of warehousing the barrels for 50 years. You&#8217;re paying decades&#8217; worth of security guards&#8217; salaries, for starters. Moreover, alcohol has an inconvenient habit of gradually evaporating, so you have to distill a lot of liquid in 2013 in order to bottle a half-century-old product in 2063.</p> <p>Makers of fine spirits give me samples in the hopes that I&#8217;ll describe them flatteringly to you, readers, because some of you do have a four- or five-figure booze budget. Without naming brands, I do encounter duds.</p> <blockquote class="pullquote"><p>Without naming brands, certain products have been aged too long and end up too woody-tasting</p></blockquote> <p>Sitting in contact with oak can make spirits like Cognac and Scotch more delicious over the decades, but spirits critics often conclude that certain products have been aged too long and end up too woody-tasting.</p> <p>That is not the case with Glenfiddich 50. Acknowledging that there will be some variation from batch to batch, my little nip of 50-year-old at an event last June was (<a href="http://life.nationalpost.com/2012/12/09/happy-hour-drinkable-gifts-for-mom-and-this-columnist/" target="_blank">as I have described in this space before</a>) sweet and smoky like a mesquite-laden charcoal barbecue that&#8217;s just been doused with water.</p> <p>There are lovely notes of orange and rose and &#8230; I&#8217;ll stop torturing myself now. I love that whisky and will buy a bottle if I ever win the lottery. My friend John Maxwell, owner of Allen&#8217;s in Toronto, waxed wistfully to the <em>Toronto Star</em> that it was &#8220;a whisky of uncommon depth and complexity.&#8221;</p> <p>Whoever ended up with the missing bottle either knew this or may be discovering it now. There&#8217;s been a hint of speculation that the alleged thief was motivated by a desire to resell the whisky on the black market.</p> <blockquote class="pullquote"><p>I love that whisky and will buy a bottle if I ever win the lottery</p></blockquote> <p>The more romantic scenario is that someone would steal a bottle of whisky that was distilled while John Diefenbaker was prime minister simply to taste it.</p> <p>Other posh whiskies from the famous Glenfiddich distillery await those who are neither super-rich nor crooks. Ontario&#8217;s liquor stores also sell a 40-year-old for a relatively modest $3,544.95. My tasting notes for that one?</p> <p>&#8220;Unfolds in the nose and on the palate with a bouquet of dark berries, smoke, warm oak and much else. What&#8217;s hardest to convey is how well structured it is, like a great burgundy,&#8221; I wrote in 2010. </p> <p>The delicious 21-year-old, available in British Columbia, is finished in ex-rum barrels and tastes like figgy, orangey, leathery drops of tropical sunshine. It can be yours for a mere $200.</p> <p>Finally, forgive me if I call Glenfiddich Distillery Edition a total steal at around $75-$85.</p> <div class="npBlock npRule npRelated"><h4
