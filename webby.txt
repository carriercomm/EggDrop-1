For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Poe_(disambiguation)" title="Poe (disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig">Poe (disambiguation)</a>.</div> <div class="thumb tright"> <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PoE_Access_Point_v2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/PoE_Access_Point_v2.jpg/300px-PoE_Access_Point_v2.jpg" width="300" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/PoE_Access_Point_v2.jpg/450px-PoE_Access_Point_v2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/PoE_Access_Point_v2.jpg/600px-PoE_Access_Point_v2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="740" data-file-height="555" /></a> <div class="thumbcaption"> <div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PoE_Access_Point_v2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div> Given a single Power over Ethernet connection (single gray cable looping below), a PoE splitter provides both data (gray cable looping above) and power (black cable also looping above) connections for a <a href="/wiki/Wireless_access_point" title="Wireless access point">wireless access point</a>. The splitter is the silver and black box in the middle, between the wiring box on the left and the access point (with its two antennas) on the right. The PoE connection eliminates the need for a nearby <a href="/wiki/Power_outlet" title="Power outlet" class="mw-redirect">power outlet</a>.</div> </div> </div> <p><b>Power over Ethernet</b> or <b>PoE</b> describes any of several standardized or ad-hoc systems which pass electrical power along with data on Ethernet cabling. This allows a single cable to provide both data connection and electrical power to devices such as <a href="/wiki/Wireless_access_point" title="Wireless access point">wireless access points</a> or <a href="/wiki/IP_camera" title="IP camera">IP cameras</a>. Unlike standards such as <a href="/wiki/Universal_Serial_Bus" title="Universal Serial Bus" class="mw-redirect">Universal Serial Bus</a> which also power devices over the data cables, PoE allows long cable lengths. Power may be carried on the same conductors as the data, or it may be carried on dedicated conductors in the same cable.</p> <p>There are several common techniques for transmitting power over Ethernet cabling. Two of them have been standardized by <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.3" title="IEEE 802.3">IEEE 802.3</a>. Since only two of the four pairs are needed for <a href="/wiki/10BASE-T" title="10BASE-T" class="mw-redirect">10BASE-T</a> or <a href="/wiki/100BASE-TX" title="100BASE-TX" class="mw-redirect">100BASE-TX</a>, power may be transmitted on the unused conductors of a cable. In the IEEE standards, this is referred to as <i>Alternative B</i>. Power may also be transmitted on the data conductors by applying a <a href="/wiki/Common-mode_signal" title="Common-mode signal">common-mode voltage</a> to each pair. Because <a href="/wiki/Ethernet_over_twisted_pair" title="Ethernet over twisted pair">twisted-pair Ethernet</a> uses <a href="/wiki/Differential_signalling" title="Differential signalling" class="mw-redirect">differential signalling</a>, this does not interfere with data transmission. The common mode voltage is easily extracted using the <a href="/wiki/Center_tap" title="Center tap">center tap</a> of the standard Ethernet <a href="/wiki/Pulse_transformer" title="Pulse transformer" class="mw-redirect">pulse transformer</a>. This is similar to the <a href="/wiki/Phantom_power" title="Phantom power">phantom power</a> technique commonly used for powering audio microphones. In the IEEE standards, this is referred to as <i>Alternative A</i>.</p> <p>In addition to standardizing existing practice for spare-pair and common-mode data pair power transmission, the IEEE PoE standards provide for signalling between the power source equipment (PSE) and powered device (PD). This signaling allows the presence of a conformant device to be detected by the power source, and allows the device and source to negotiate the amount of power required or available. Up to a theoretical 51 watts is available for a device, depending on the version of the standard in use and the vendor of the hardware.</p> <p></p> <div id="toc" class="toc"> <div id="toctitle"> </div> <p></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_development">Standard development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_over_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Standard development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>The IEEE standard for PoE requires <a href="/wiki/Category_5_cable" title="Category 5 cable">category 5 cable</a> or higher for high power levels, but can operate with <a href="/wiki/Category_3_cable" title="Category 3 cable">category 3 cable</a> if less power is required.<sup id="cite_ref-33.1.1c_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33.1.1c-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Power is supplied in <a href="/wiki/Common-mode_signal" title="Common-mode signal">common mode</a> over two or more of the <a href="/wiki/Twisted_pair" title="Twisted pair">differential pairs</a> of wires found in the <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> cables and comes from a power supply within a PoE-enabled networking device such as an <a href="/wiki/Ethernet_switch" title="Ethernet switch" class="mw-redirect">Ethernet switch</a> or can be <i>injected</i> into a cable run with a <i>midspan</i> power supply.</p> <p>The original <b>IEEE 802.3af-2003</b><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> PoE standard provides up to <span class="nowrap">15.4 W</span> of <a href="/wiki/Direct_Current" title="Direct Current" class="mw-redirect">DC</a> power (minimum <span class="nowrap">44 V DC</span> and <span class="nowrap">350 mA</span><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup>) to each device.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Only <span class="nowrap">12.95 W</span> is assured to be available at the powered device as some power dissipates in the cable.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>The updated <b>IEEE 802.3at-2009</b><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> PoE standard also known as <b>PoE+</b> or <b>PoE plus</b>, provides up to <span class="nowrap">25.5 W</span> of power.<sup id="cite_ref-standards.ieee.org_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-standards.ieee.org-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The 2009 standard prohibits a powered device from using all four pairs for power.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Some vendors have announced products that claim to be compatible with the 802.3at standard and offer up to <span class="nowrap">51 W</span> of power over a single cable by utilizing all four pairs in the Category 5 cable.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>Both of these amendments have since been incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.3" title="IEEE 802.3">IEEE 802.3-2012</a> publication.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_other_integrated_data_and_power_standards">Comparison with other integrated data and power standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_over_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Comparison with other integrated data and power standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>PoE provides both data and power connections in one cable, so equipment doesn't require a separate cable for each need. For equipment that does not already have a power or data connection, PoE can be attractive when the power demand is modest. For example, PoE is useful for <a href="/wiki/VoIP_phone" title="VoIP phone">IP telephones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wireless_access_point" title="Wireless access point">wireless access points</a>, <a href="/wiki/Video_camera" title="Video camera">cameras</a> with <a href="/wiki/PTZ_camera" title="PTZ camera" class="mw-redirect">pan tilt and zoom</a> (PTZ), and remote <a href="/wiki/Network_switch" title="Network switch">Ethernet switches</a>. PoE can provide long cable runs e.g. 100&#160;m (330&#160;ft) and deliver 12&#160;W of <a href="/wiki/Galvanic_isolation" title="Galvanic isolation">galvanically isolated</a> power.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> PoE-plus provides even more power.</p> <p>There are competing data and power technologies. The <a href="/wiki/Universal_Serial_Bus" title="Universal Serial Bus" class="mw-redirect">Universal Serial Bus</a> (USB) provides both data and power, but it is designed for short cables with a maximum length of 5&#160;m (16&#160;ft) and provides less than 2.5&#160;W of non-isolated power. It is less expensive than PoE, and works well for low power peripherals such as a <a href="/wiki/Mouse_(computer)" title="Mouse (computer)" class="mw-redirect">computer mouse</a>, a headset/microphone or a serial port. Some peripherals, such as speakers, scanners and printers, need more power than USB can provide. <a href="/wiki/IEEE_1394" title="IEEE 1394">IEEE 1394</a> (FireWire) is similar to USB but can provide substantially more power (<span class="nowrap">45 W</span>) at a distance of <span class="nowrap">4.5 m</span>.</p> <p>If a device already has power available but no data link, then PoE may not be attractive. A wireless data connection such as <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.11" title="IEEE 802.11">IEEE 802.11</a> may be more economical than running a data cable for the device. Alternatively, there are <a href="/wiki/Power_line_communication" title="Power line communication" class="mw-redirect">power line communication</a> technologies that can use power cables for transmitting data. Using some power line modems may be more economical than running a cable.</p> <p>When data rate and power requirements are both low, other approaches may be viable. <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phones" title="Mobile phones" class="mw-redirect">Mobile phones</a>, for example, use batteries for power and antennas for communication. Remote weather sensors use very low data rates, so batteries (sometimes supplemented with solar power) and custom wireless data links are used.</p> <p>Depending on the application, some of the advantages with PoE over other technologies may be:</p> <ul> <li>Inexpensive cabling carries both data and power</li> <li>Power to equipment can be remotely cycled</li> <li>Fast data rate</li> </ul> The maximum allowed continuous output power per cable in IEEE 802.3af is 15.40&#160;W. A later specification, IEEE 802.3at, offers 25.50&#160;W.</p> <p>When the device is a switch, it is commonly called an endspan (although IEEE 802.3af refers to it as endpoint). Otherwise, if it's an intermediary device between a non PoE capable switch and a PoE device, it's called a midspan. An external PoE <i>injector</i> is a <i>midspan</i> device<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Powered_device">Powered device</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_over_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Powered device">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>A powered device (PD) is a device powered by a PSE and thus consumes energy. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Wireless_access_point" title="Wireless access point">wireless access points</a>, <a href="/wiki/VoIP_phone" title="VoIP phone">IP Phones</a>, and <a href="/wiki/IP_camera" title="IP camera">IP Cameras</a>.</p> <p>Many powered devices have an auxiliary power connector for an optional, external, power supply. Depending on the PD design, some, none, or all power can be supplied from the auxiliary port,<sup id="cite_ref-802.3-2008.2C_33.3.5_PD_Power_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-802.3-2008.2C_33.3.5_PD_Power-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-802.3at-2009.2C_33.3.7_PD_Power_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-802.3at-2009.2C_33.3.7_PD_Power-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> with the auxiliary port sometimes acting as backup power in case of PoE supplied power failure.</p> (October 2013)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> expect PoE to become a global longterm DC power cabling standard and replace "<a href="/wiki/Wall_wart" title="Wall wart" class="mw-redirect">wall wart</a>" converters, which cannot be easily centrally managed, waste energy, are often poorly designed, and are easily vulnerable to damage from <a href="/wiki/Voltage_spike" title="Voltage spike">surges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">brownouts</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> <p>Critics of this approach argue that DC power is inherently less efficient than AC power due to the lower voltage, and this is made worse by the thin conductors of Ethernet. A typical 48-port Ethernet switch has a 50&#160;W to 80&#160;W power supply allocated for the traditional Ethernet switch and transceiver IC. Over and above this it requires typically a 740&#160;W (for 802.3af) to 1480&#160;W (for 802.3at) power supply allocated solely for PoE ports, permitting a maximum draw on each. This can be quite inefficient to supply through long cables. However, where this central supply replaces several dedicated AC circuits, transformers and inverters, and prevents expensive human interventions (AC installations) the power loss of long thin DC cable is easily justifiable. Power can always be introduced on the device end of the Ethernet cable (radically improving efficiency) where AC power is available. Other challenges of POE include power failure and short circuiting power-data wires from a crushed network cable or mispunched rj-45 wall jack. This could possibly transfer a 44 volt POE voltage into a 2 volt data wire inadvertently resulting in equipment failure and data loss. The issue of heat generation typically generated remotely at the end devices is now transferred into the DataRoom Switch with increased heat dissipation within the datacenter room altering the BTU cooling requirement specifications as well as the power consumption.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Switch_power_features">Switch power features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_over_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Switch power features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>The switches themselves often contain "active", "smart", or "managed" power management features to reduce AC draw of all devices involved.</p> <p>Multi-protocol teaming standards (<a href="/wiki/G.9960" title="G.9960" class="mw-redirect">G.9960</a>, <a href="/wiki/G.hn" title="G.hn">G.hn</a>, and <a href="/wiki/IEEE_P1905" title="IEEE P1905" class="mw-redirect">IEEE P1905</a>) and handoff standards (<a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.21" title="IEEE 802.21">IEEE 802.21</a>) generally rely on simulating Ethernet features in other media.</p> <p>By late 2011, some of the energy management features are proprietary.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Advertising for power-over-Ethernet devices usually cites its "green" features including less packaging and improvements over previous models.</p> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Integrating_EEE_and_PoE">Integrating EEE and PoE</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_over_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Integrating EEE and PoE">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>After integration with the <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.3az" title="IEEE 802.3az" class="mw-redirect">IEEE 802.3az</a> <a href="/wiki/Energy-Efficient_Ethernet" title="Energy-Efficient Ethernet">Energy-Efficient Ethernet</a> (EEE) standard, the energy management capabilities of the combined standard are expected to be good. Pre-standard integrations of EEE and PoE (such as <a href="/wiki/Marvell_Technology_Group" title="Marvell Technology Group">Marvell</a>'s <b>EEPoE</b> outlined in a May 2011 white paper<sup id="cite_ref-marvell1_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marvell1-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup>) claim to achieve a savings upwards of 3 watts per link, extremely significant across the tens of millions of new links shipped each year. These losses are especially significant as higher power devices come online. Marvell claims that:</p> <dl> <dd>"With the evolution of PoE from a fairly low power source (up to 12.95W per port) to one with devices of up to 25.5W, the direct current (DC) power losses over Ethernet cables increased exponentially. Approximately 4.5W/port of power is wasted on a CAT5, CAT5e, CAT6 or CAT6A cable...after 100m... EEE typically saves no more than 1W per link, so addressing the 4.5W per link loss from PoE transmission inefficiency would provide much more incremental savings. New energy-efficient PoE (EEPoE) technology can change increase efficiency to 94% while transmitting over the same 25ohm cable, powering IEEE 802.3at-compliant devices in synchronous 4-pairs. When utilizing synchronous 4-pairs, powered devices are fed using all the available wires. For example, on a 24-port IEEE 802.3at-2009 Type 2 system (delivering 25.5W per port), more than 50W are saved."<sup id="cite_ref-marvell1_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marvell1-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></dd> </dl> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_implementation">Standard implementation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_over_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Standard implementation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>Standards-based Power over Ethernet is implemented following the specifications in IEEE 802.3af-2003 (which was later incorporated as clause 33 into <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.3-2005" title="IEEE 802.3-2005" class="mw-redirect">IEEE 802.3-2005</a>) or the 2009 update, IEEE 802.3at. A <a href="/wiki/Phantom_power" title="Phantom power">phantom power</a> technique is used to allow the powered pairs to also carry data. This permits its use not only with <a href="/wiki/10BASE-T" title="10BASE-T" class="mw-redirect">10BASE-T</a> and <a href="/wiki/100BASE-TX" title="100BASE-TX" class="mw-redirect">100BASE-TX</a>, which use only two of the four pairs in the cable, but also with <a href="/wiki/1000BASE-T" title="1000BASE-T" class="mw-redirect">1000BASE-T</a> (gigabit Ethernet), which uses all four pairs for data transmission. This is possible because all versions of Ethernet over twisted pair cable specify <a href="/wiki/Differential_signaling" title="Differential signaling">differential data transmission</a> over each pair with <a href="/wiki/Network_isolator" title="Network isolator">transformer coupling</a>; the DC supply and load connections can be made to the transformer center-taps at each end. Each pair thus operates in <a href="/wiki/Common-mode_signal" title="Common-mode signal">common mode</a> as one side of the DC supply, so two pairs are required to complete the circuit. The polarity of the DC supply may be inverted by <a href="/wiki/Ethernet_crossover_cable" title="Ethernet crossover cable">crossover cables</a>; the powered device must operate with either pair: spare pairs 4–5 and 7–8 or data pairs 1–2 and 3–6. Polarity is required on data pairs, and ambiguously implemented for spare pairs, with the use of a <a href="/wiki/Diode_bridge" title="Diode bridge">diode bridge</a>.</p> <table class="wikitable"> class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_over_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Powering devices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>Two modes, A and B, are available. Mode A delivers power on the data pairs of 100BASE-TX or 10BASE-T. Mode B delivers power on the spare pairs. PoE can also be used on 1000BASE-T Ethernet, in which case there are no spare pairs and all power is delivered using the phantom technique.</p> <p>Mode A has two alternate configurations (MDI and MDI-X), using the same pairs but with different polarities. In mode A, pins 1 and 2 (pair #2 in <a href="/wiki/T568B" title="T568B" class="mw-redirect">T568B</a> wiring) form one side of the 48&#160;V DC, and pins 3 and 6 (pair #3 in T568B) form the other side. These are the same two pairs used for data transmission in 10BASE-T and 100BASE-TX, allowing the provision of both power and data over only two pairs in such networks. The free polarity allows PoE to accommodate for crossover cables, patch cables and <a href="/wiki/Auto-MDIX" title="Auto-MDIX" class="mw-redirect">auto-MDIX</a>.</p> <p>In mode B, pins 4–5 (pair #1 in both <a href="/wiki/T568A" title="T568A" class="mw-redirect">T568A</a> and T568B) form one side of the DC supply and pins 7–8 (pair #4 in both T568A and T568B) provide the return; these are the "spare" pairs in 10BASE-T and 100BASE-TX. Mode B, therefore, requires a 4-pair cable.</p> <p>The PSE (<a href="#Power_sourcing_equipment">power sourcing equipment</a>), not the PD (<a href="#Powered_device">powered device</a>), decides whether power mode A or B shall be used. PDs that implement only Mode A or Mode B are disallowed by the standard.</p> <p>The PSE can implement mode A or B or both. A PD indicates that it is standards-compliant by placing a 25&#160;kΩ resistor between the powered pairs. If the PSE detects a resistance that is too high or too low (including a short circuit), no power is applied. This protects devices that do not support PoE. An optional "power class" feature allows the PD to indicate its power requirements by changing the sense resistance at higher voltages. To stay powered, the PD must continuously use 5–10&#160;mA for at least 60&#160;ms with no more than 400&#160;ms since last use or else it will be unpowered by the PSE.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>There are two types of PSEs: endspans and midspans. Endspans (commonly called PoE switches) are Ethernet switches that include the power over Ethernet transmission circuitry. Midspans are power injectors that stand between a regular Ethernet switch and the powered device, injecting power without affecting the data.</p> <p>Endspans are normally used on new installations or when the switch has to be replaced for other reasons (such as moving from <a href="/wiki/10/100" title="10/100" class="mw-redirect">10/100</a>&#160;Mbit/s to 1&#160;Gbit/s or adding security protocols), which makes it convenient to add the PoE capability. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cisco">Cisco</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_over_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cisco">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>Cisco manufactured WLAN access points and <a href="/wiki/VoIP_phone" title="VoIP phone">IP phones</a> many years before there was an IEEE standard for delivering PoE. Cisco's original PoE implementation is not software upgradeable to the IEEE 802.3af standard. Cisco's original PoE equipment was capable of delivering up to <span class="nowrap">10 W</span> per port. The amount of power to be delivered is negotiated between the endpoint and the Cisco switch based on a power value that was added to the Cisco proprietary <a href="/wiki/Cisco_Discovery_Protocol" title="Cisco Discovery Protocol">Cisco Discovery Protocol</a> (CDP). CDP is also responsible for dynamically communicating the Voice VLAN value from the Cisco switch to the Cisco IP Phone.</p> <p>Under Cisco's pre-standard scheme, the PSE (switch) will send a <a href="/wiki/Autonegotiation#Electrical_signals" title="Autonegotiation">Fast Link Pulse</a> (FLP) on the transmit pair. The PD (device) connects the transmit line to the receive line via a <a href="/wiki/Low-pass_filter" title="Low-pass filter">low pass filter</a>. And thus the PSE gets the FLP in return. And a common mode current between pair 1 and 2 will be provided resulting in <span class="nowrap">48 V DC</span><sup id="cite_ref-ciscopress_ciscophone_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ciscopress_ciscophone-31"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> and <span class="nowrap">6.3 W</span><sup id="cite_ref-conticomp_CAT6500POE_ds_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conticomp_CAT6500POE_ds-32"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> default of allocated power. The PD has then to provide Ethernet link within <span class="nowrap">5 seconds</span> to the auto-negotiation mode switch port. A later CDP message with a <a href="/wiki/Type-length-value" title="Type-length-value">type-length-value</a> tells the PSE its final power requirement. A discontinued link pulses shuts down power.<sup id="cite_ref-cisco_technote00_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cisco_technote00-33"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>In 2014, Cisco created another non-standard PoE implementation called Universal Power over Ethernet (UPOE). UPOE can use all 4 pairs, after negotiation, to supply up to 60&#160;W.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PowerDsine">PowerDsine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_over_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: PowerDsine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>PowerDsine, now a <a href="/wiki/Microsemi" title="Microsemi">Microsemi</a> brand, have been selling midspan power injectors since 1999 with its proprietary <i>Power over LAN</i> solution. Several companies such as <a href="/wiki/Polycom" title="Polycom">Polycom</a>, <a href="/wiki/3Com" title="3Com">3Com</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lucent" title="Lucent">Lucent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nortel" title="Nortel">Nortel</a> utilize PowerDsine's Power over LAN.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Passive">Passive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_over_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Passive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>Most passive applications use the pinout of 802.3af mode B - with DC plus on pins 4 and 5 and DC minus on 7 and 8 (see chart below). Data is then on 1-2 and 3-6. This limits operation to 100Mbit/s. Gigabit passive injectors use a transformer on the data pins to allow power and data to share the cable and is typically compatible with 802.3af Mode A. In the common "passive" PoE system, the injector does not communicate with the powered device to negotiate its wattage requirements, but merely supplies power at all times. Passive midspan injectors up to 12 ports simplify installations. Devices needing 5&#160;Volts cannot use PoE at 5&#160;V on Ethernet cable beyond about 15 feet (4.6&#160;m) due to IR loss, so a 24&#160;V or 48&#160;V to 5&#160;V DC-DC converter is required at the remote end.<sup id="cite_ref-Active_5v_Splitter_manufacturer_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Active_5v_Splitter_manufacturer-36"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> Passive DC-to-DC injectors also exist which convert a 9&#160;V to 36&#160;V DC input power source to a stabilized 24&#160;V 1&#160;A or 48&#160;V 0.5&#160;A PoE feed with '+' on pins 4 &amp; 5 and '−' on pins 7 &amp; 8. These DC-to-DC PoE injectors are used in various telecom applications.<sup id="cite_ref-Passive_PoE_injector_manufacturer_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Passive_PoE_injector_manufacturer-37"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_capacity_limits">Power capacity limits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_over_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Power capacity limits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p><a href="/wiki/Category_5_cable" title="Category 5 cable">Category 5 cable</a> uses 24 <a href="/wiki/American_wire_gauge" title="American wire gauge">AWG</a> conductors, which can safely carry 360&#160;mA at 50&#160;V according to the latest <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_Industry_Association" title="Telecommunications Industry Association">TIA</a> ruling.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The cable has eight conductors (only half of which are used for power) and therefore the absolute maximum power transmitted using direct current is <span class="nowrap">50 V × 0.360 A × 2 = 36 W</span>. Considering the voltage drop after 100&#160;m, a PD would be able to receive 31.6&#160;W. The additional heat generated in the wires by PoE at this current level (4.4 watts per 100 meter cable) limits the total number of cables in a bundle to be 100 cables at 45&#160;°C, according to the <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_Industry_Association" title="Telecommunications Industry Association">TIA</a>. This can be somewhat alleviated by the use of <a href="/wiki/Category_6_cable" title="Category 6 cable">Category 6 cable</a> which uses 23 AWG conductors.</p> <table class="wikitable">
