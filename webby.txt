<p><i><b>Dinuguan</b></i> (in <a href="/wiki/Visayan_languages" title="Visayan languages">Visayan</a>, also called <i>dinardaraan</i> in <a href="/wiki/Ilocano_language" title="Ilocano language" class="mw-redirect">Ilocano</a>, <i>tid-tad</i> in <a href="/wiki/Pampanga" title="Pampanga">Pampanga</a>, <i>dugo-dugo</i> in <a href="/wiki/Cebuano_language" title="Cebuano language">Cebuano</a>, <i>sinugaok</i> in <a href="/wiki/Batangas" title="Batangas">Batangas</a>, <i>rugodugo</i> in <a href="/wiki/Waray-Waray_language" title="Waray-Waray language">Waray</a>, and <i>sampayna</i> or <i>champayna</i> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Mindanao" title="Northern Mindanao">Northern Mindanao</a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Filipino</a> <a href="/wiki/Basic_taste#Savouriness" title="Basic taste" class="mw-redirect">savory</a> <a href="/wiki/Stew" title="Stew">stew</a> of meat and/or <a href="/wiki/Offal" title="Offal">offal</a> (typically lungs, kidneys, intestines, ears, heart and snout) <a href="/wiki/Simmering" title="Simmering">simmered</a> in a rich, spicy dark <a href="/wiki/Gravy" title="Gravy">gravy</a> of pig <a href="/wiki/Blood_as_food" title="Blood as food">blood</a>, garlic, chili (most often <i><a href="/wiki/Siling_mahaba" title="Siling mahaba">siling mahaba</a></i>), and vinegar.<sup id="cite_ref-cui_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cui-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The term <i>dinuguan</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Filipino_language" title="Filipino language">Filipino</a> word <i>dugo</i> meaning "blood". Possible <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> translations include <b>pork blood stew</b> or <b>blood pudding stew</b>. It is also sometimes jokingly called <b>chocolate meat</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-ign_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>It is frequently considered an unusual or alarming dish to foreigners though it is rather similar to European-style <a href="/wiki/Blood_sausage" title="Blood sausage">blood sausage</a>, or British <a href="/wiki/Black_pudding" title="Black pudding">black pudding</a> in a saucy stew form.<sup id="cite_ref-dav_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dav-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It is perhaps closer in appearance and preparation to the <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> soup <a href="/wiki/Czernina" title="Czernina">Czernina</a> or an even more ancient <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Spartan</a> dish known as <i>melas zomos</i> (<a href="/wiki/Black_soup" title="Black soup">black soup</a>) whose primary ingredients were pork, vinegar and blood.</p> <p>Dinuguan can also be served without using any <a href="/wiki/Offal" title="Offal">offal</a>, using only choice cuts of pork. In Batangas, this version is known as <i>sinungaok</i>. It can also be made from beef and chicken meat, the latter being known as <i>dinuguang manok</i> ('chicken dinuguan').<sup id="cite_ref-dav_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dav-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-phob_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phob-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Dinuguan</i> is usually served with <a href="/wiki/White_rice" title="White rice">white rice</a> or a Philippine rice cake called <a href="/wiki/Puto" title="Puto">puto</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dav_3-2"
