                   <div class="body">                                                      <p>Februaries are big for Kate Mara. Take last year: On an unassuming weekend in the dark heart of winter, Netflix dropped the first season of&nbsp;<em>House of Cards</em>&nbsp;introducing viewers to Zoe Barnes, the slickly ambitious reporter who represents the third point of Congressman Francis Underwood&rsquo;s vindictive triangle offense. (The second: Underwood&rsquo;s wife, played by Robin Wright.) Keeping up with Wright and Kevin Spacey is big work, and yet, not a half hour into the first episode, Zoe kisses off a date who wants an invite upstairs:&nbsp; &ldquo;Oh&hellip;you&rsquo;re so sweet.&hellip; But if I was going to fuck you, you&rsquo;d know.&rdquo; The sting of that slap lasts for a season.</p><p>Mara is tall enough to ride roller coasters, but barely. She&rsquo;s acted way longer than younger sister Rooney, but doesn&rsquo;t seem overly impressed with either of their ascents.&nbsp; She seems to consider&nbsp;<em>HoC</em>&nbsp;not some zenith but another hilltop in a steady career that includes&nbsp;<em>Brokeback Mountain</em>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<em>127 Hours.</em>&nbsp;As for that run of very excellent Februaries? It dates back nine seasons, not just two. Mara is NFL royalty: Mom&rsquo;s family runs the Steelers; Dad&rsquo;s family runs the Giants. That&rsquo;s five Super Bowl Sundays since 2006. &ldquo;We dressed up for Giants games like we were dressing up for church,&rdquo; she says. &ldquo;It&rsquo;s our family&nbsp;<em>business.</em>&rdquo; What about the less-than-stellar seasons both teams just had? &ldquo;It was bound to happen. It&rsquo;s been too amazing. Of course losing sucked.&rdquo;</p><p>Just before Christmas, she picked a spot to meet near her gym in Silver Lake. She wore a black leather moto jacket over a Giants workout tee. After she ordered a smoothie with apple, kale, and lemon, a waiter tried to convince her that lemon would suck with apple and kale. &ldquo;Well, it doesn&rsquo;t. I mean, I get it every day. Don&rsquo;t judge my smoothie.&rdquo; The salt in her responses reminded me of someone else. Someone used to being called &ldquo;Twitter twat.&rdquo;</p><p><strong>When do you give up on your teams?</strong><br />I&rsquo;m an eternal optimist.</p><p><strong>What about when the Giants start 0-6?</strong><br /> Never. Besides, the Giants have a history of turning things around. Next year we could go to the Super Bowl. One of the greatest things about football is, unlike baseball, you really don&rsquo;t know what&rsquo;s going to happen at the start of the season.</p><p><strong>Do you get home to New York much?</strong><br /> Yeah, my whole family&rsquo;s still there. My dad&rsquo;s mom is still very much in charge of the family. My dad&rsquo;s one of eleven&mdash;I have about 50 first cousins on the Mara side.</p><p><strong>What&rsquo;s your grandma like in the owner&rsquo;s box? Does she curse at players?</strong><br /> Oh, she would never curse. No, no, no. I mean, she&rsquo;s tough. She&rsquo;s a really tough lady. But she&rsquo;s also very proper. So there&rsquo;s no cursing.</p><p><strong>Does the wine go a little faster in a tight fourth quarter?</strong><br /> I don&rsquo;t think there&rsquo;s any drinking involved.</p><p><strong>No way.</strong><br /> I mean, if we win, or if we&rsquo;ve lost, maybe people drink after the game, but, like, it&rsquo;s funny, because obviously drinking is such a massive part of most people&rsquo;s football-watching experience, but to me, it&rsquo;s such a foreign thing. It was like going to the office. Everyone was supposed to look their best. Obviously as we got older, that kind of shifted, and now we can wear a Giants jersey. But that goes with the drinking&mdash;it just wasn&rsquo;t something&hellip;</p><p><strong>It&rsquo;s business.</strong><br /> Right.</p><p><strong>Sunday mornings with a one o&rsquo;clock game&hellip; How early does the whip-cracking start to get everyone ready?</strong><br /> My mom&rsquo;s always late and my dad&rsquo;s always early. I&rsquo;m like my dad. Obviously Tom Coughlin wasn&rsquo;t our coach when I was kid, but now it&rsquo;s called Coughlin Time. If you&rsquo;re not there fifteen minutes early, you&rsquo;re late. My dad&rsquo;s ready an hour early sitting in the kitchen by the door, looking at the clock. I don&rsquo;t blame him.</p><p><strong>Do you try to get home in the fall?</strong><br /> During football season, I try to spend as much time in New York as possible. At first it was very strange not waking up on Sunday with my family. But then I was like, <em>Oh, I can just get on a plane.</em></p><p><strong>Some of my friends went to your high school, same time as your brother and sister. They call Rooney &ldquo;Tricia.&rdquo;</strong><br /> See, then they definitely did&mdash;they&rsquo;re not lying.</p><p><strong>When you&rsquo;re with your sister, do you talk about acting or anything but acting?</strong><br /> Oh no, talking about acting isn&rsquo;t like, &ldquo;Ugh, God, we have to talk about acting, how boring!&rdquo; It&rsquo;s like a passion. It&rsquo;s not, you know, some burden. Of course we do.</p><p><strong>Speaking of acting: Does the new season of <em>House of Cards </em>turn up the dials?</strong><br /> I mean, the dials were <em>really</em> high in the first season. Beau Willimon has a very big imagination. And he and our writing staff, I think they really pulled through this season. Because it&rsquo;s hard to top all the things that happened last season. Last year, before Season One came out, I had seen all the episodes; this year I haven&rsquo;t, so I&rsquo;m just as excited to watch it like anyone else. Still, I think it&#39;s just as surprising and addictive. Hopefully, we&#39;ll see.</p><p><strong>Did you, like, drop in on Politico before you started filming?</strong><br /> I didn&rsquo;t shadow anyone. Any questions I had I went to Beau. But being a journalist wasn&rsquo;t the most important thing for me. That was just her job&mdash;it could be anything. The most important aspect of Zoe that I felt I needed to understand and wrap my head around was her crazy drive and ambition. Sitting around with Beau and Fincher and Kevin, just setting the backstories&mdash;that&rsquo;s where I got all of my information.</p><p><strong>When you shot Season 2, did you have to put everything else aside?</strong><br /> I mean, I&rsquo;m sure Kevin has to, because he&rsquo;s in almost every scene. But for everyone else, it&rsquo;s very different. I don&rsquo;t shoot every day. I actually shot <em>Transcendence</em> while I was shooting <em>House of Cards</em>. So I wasn&rsquo;t even in Baltimore the whole time. I was in New Mexico.</p><p><strong>With Johnny Depp.</strong><br /> We were shooting in the middle of the desert in the summer, in New Mexico, and it was 110 degrees. Just because I felt so much in that world, even though our movie is not that.</p><p><strong>Between <em>House of Cards</em> and <em>Transcendence</em>, you&rsquo;re in a comfortable spot. Are you gonna move back to New York for good now?</strong><br /> I hope one day I&rsquo;ll have a place there. But I really love being able to do both. I&rsquo;m not one of those people who hates on L.A. I love it.</p><p><strong>If you were not an actress, would you still be living in L.A.?</strong><br /> I don&rsquo;t know how I would have come to L.A. if I wasn&rsquo;t an actress.</p><p><strong>Well, I mean, lots of people do. You couldn&rsquo;t have ended up here doing something else?</strong><br /> No.</p><p><strong>You would&rsquo;ve been somewhere else?</strong><br /> Maybe I would have visited L.A for some random reason, but the only reason I came here at nineteen was for work.
