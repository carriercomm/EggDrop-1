format-standard hentry category-comestibles tag-favorite tag-lagavulin tag-scotch tag-talisker">   <header class="entry-header">          <h1 class="entry-title"><a href="http://upsidownia.com/how-to-drink-scotch/" title="Permalink to How and Why to Drink Scotch" rel="bookmark">How and Why to Drink Scotch</a></h1>        <div class="entry-meta">          <span class="author-link">       Posted by       <a href="http://upsidownia.com/author/nava00/" rel="author">        nava00      </a>      </span><!-- .author-link -->        </div><!-- .entry-meta -->   </header><!-- .entry-header -->    <div class="entry-content">      <p><em>(written while wearing a silk robe and sitting in a red velvet chair with a gilt-inscribed copy of Proust and a whisky glass of Talisker 175 on the table near my glasses)</em></p> <p><em>In a faintly British accent:</em></p> <p><em>There are few things in life which bring greater comfort to a man than single malt whisky. (takes a sip &#8220;Mm, ahhh&#8221;) It is warming, calming and it never leaves you with the clap.  Indeed, we were never truly civilized before the good Scotsman perfected his potion. For virility and courage, for culling that wise face and shrewd eyes  there be no equal tincture, no better elixir.</em></p> <p><em> </em></p> <p><em></em>So you&#8217;re trying to cultivate that wasp-y look and lifestyle but you can&#8217;t stand the taste of scotch or you don&#8217;t know what kind to get.</p> <p>Or maybe you had your first divine sip at a soiree and you&#8217;re wondering where to go from here.</p> <p>I&#8217;m here to tell you what I love about scotch and which ones I drink.</p> <p>&nbsp;</p> <p>Time was, I couldn&#8217;t stand the stuff. The smoky smell and the harsh alcohol taste really scared me away and I never drank scotch in college, when everyone was getting into it.</p> <p>I began to realize how good scotch is when I learned how to sip slowly. When you drink scotch you want to sip it more slowly than anything you&#8217;ve ever sipped before &#8211; much slower than you sip wine.</p> <p><strong>How to Learn to Like Scotch</strong></p> <p>For your first experiment I recommend you get a bottle of Talisker 10. The bottle costs about $50 which is on the low side for good scotch. If it&#8217;s your first bottle and you&#8217;re willing to sink in a bit more, you should get something very smooth like the Talisker 25 which is a hefty $200 but worth every penny.</p> <p>Pour yourself a half shot and then take a tiny tiny sip so that the drink doesn&#8217;t even go past your tongue. Allow the scotch to sit in a little puddle on your tongue. Close your mouth. Breath. Do you taste that?</p> <p>Repeat with more tiny sips and take your time. And I mean that really. Don&#8217;t take another sip until you feel like it. Let the flavors fill up your mouth and nose and swirl around.</p> <p>If you really don&#8217;t like sipping the scotch then stop here. Sometimes you&#8217;re just not in the zone, so try again tomorrow. But don&#8217;t give up: you&#8217;ve already sunk good money into this.</p> <p>If you&#8217;re ready for the next step though, fill up your glass again and take bigger sips. Allow small amounts to go down your throat. Figure out which parts of your tongue react to the flavors. Experiment. Enjoy. Go buy your next bottle. You&#8217;re on your way.</p> <p>&nbsp;</p> <p><strong>Describing Scotch</strong></p> <p>Now, a lot of people insist that unless we put names to flavors, we can&#8217;t really recognize or remember them. These people are usually in the wine business and they make up words like &#8220;buttery, chewy, raisiny&#8221; and all sorts of other nonsense words to describe flavors. (confession: sometimes I do this too. It&#8217;s kinda fun!)</p> <p>I don&#8217;t think that all of that is necessary when you drink scotch. Just enjoy. You don&#8217;t have to make up words to differentiate one scotch from another, you will just be able to recognize the different tastes and figure out what kinds you like without having to ascribe words to them. The words we use to distinguish tastes are the <i>names</i> of the scotch we&#8217;re describing.<em><br /> </em></p> <p>But there are three words that people sometimes use when it comes to scotch</p> <p>1. Smooth. So what does smooth mean? Actually, something very specific. When you say a whisky is smooth you mean that it goes down easy. That is, there is very little burn, or acid or curling of your tongue or cheek nerves. It&#8217;s that the taste is so sophisticated it doesn&#8217;t bother your mouth and throat&#8217;s normal instinctive reaction to alcohol.</p> <p>It&#8217;s hard to describe but very easy to demonstrate. Once you get your Talisker 10 (or 25), take a sip of it and then immediately compare with a sip of something common: like Maker&#8217;s Mark or something. You&#8217;ll see what <em>smooth</em> is. Smooth is something that happens to a whiskey with age. The older the whiskey, the smoother it will be, in general.</p> <p>2. Smoky- they&#8217;re referring to a flavor that is similar in sensation to smoked meat, or smoked whatever. I prefer not to use this term because I absolutely <em>HATE</em> smoked foods. I cannot stand the flavor and I categorically avoid them. On the other hand I love scotch. Especially when the scotch has a certain smokiness to it. I don&#8217;t know of a better way to describe it but the analogy makes me really unhappy.</p> <p>3. Peaty- This is similar to smoky- like eating something burnt. These things are hard to describe. You need someone to hand you a glass of Laphroaig and say &#8220;this is peaty&#8221;.</p> <p>&nbsp;</p> <p><strong>My Favorite Scotches</strong></p> <p>My first favorite scotch was Talisker 25. We went through 5 bottles of it before I was willing to try a new kind. I loved how I was able to recognize the Talisker taste instantly. Finally, I was ready to move on.</p> <p>My next brand was Lagavulin, I think it was the 16 year bottle. It was excellent and we went through it pretty quickly.</p> <p>By now I&#8217;ve tried about 10 different scotches seriously (meaning, drank it frequently) and I&#8217;ve tried about 30 in total. I go to a bar to try out new scotches. If I don&#8217;t like how a scotch tastes the first time I try it, I never buy the bottle.</p> <p>Here are the Scotches that I really really love</p> <ul> <li><span style="line-height: 13px;">Ardbeg- Any vintage and also the &#8220;Galileo&#8221; edition which was superb.</span></li> <li>Talisker- anything above 12 year is good. 10 year is pretty good but not great. The new &#8220;175&#8243; is excellent and a great price.</li> <li>Lagavulin- I&#8217;ve only had the 16 year and boy is it good.</li> <li>Laphroaig- not for the beginner! It took me a while to really get into the Laphroaigs-  they&#8217;re extremely peaty!</li> </ul> <p>Here are some that I&#8217;ve tried and I like,</p> <ul> <li>Yamazaki- this is a Japanese &#8220;Scotch&#8221; (Scotch has to be made in Scotland. But more than that, they&#8217;re not really <em>made </em>elsewhere) I&#8217;m not a huge fan of Yamazaki- in fact I never drink it if there&#8217;s something else. But Ben likes it and it&#8217;s quite inexpensive</li> <li>Bruichladdich- This is a new one for me. Our friend Alex brought it over one day. I&#8217;m still not sure about it but I haven&#8217;t rejected it.</li> </ul> <p>Here are some I don&#8217;t like:</p> <ul> <li>Blended Scotch: Avoid. Only get single malts. Some of the better-known blended brands include: Cutty Sark, Dewars, Johnnie Walker,  J&amp;B and Chivas Regal. They tend to be cheaper than single malts, but are not worth the glass they are stored in. The worst offender is Johnnie Walker, who makes overpriced swill, fooling only teenagers and the noobiest of drinkers. Their &#8220;blue label&#8221; costs close to $200 and tastes like it should cost $30.</li> <li>MacGavin&#8217;s- Ben was in a cheap mood, so he picked up the cheapest bottle in the store. It&#8217;s a single malt. It costs $20 in Manhattan. It&#8217;s been on our shelf for years and we keep it as a joke and as a reminder not to be cheap.</li> </ul> <p>You can get most of these for less than $100 and many of them for less than $50 if you know where to shop. You don&#8217;t have to spend a fortune to get a great bottle which will last for many drinks. If you live in New York you should visit Warehouse Liquors on Broadway and 8th Street. They often have killer deals.</p> <p><strong>Why to Drink Scotch </strong></p> <p>Drink scotch because once you do it will become a lifelong hobby. It will certainly enhance your evenings, especially if it accompanies your favorite hobbies such as listening to music or blogging. There is no food, or drink, or inhalant out there that is more deeply satisfying than your favorite scotch.</p> <p>A glass of good scotch is so awesome, you won&#8217;t need to get drunk off it. You will meet really cool people in the scotch section of the liquor store and you will feel really great displaying it in your house.</p> <p>Last of all, you will be able to teach your friends how to drink slowly and recognize flavors that they like.</p> <p>Cheers!</p> <p>&nbsp;</p> <p>If you drink scotch a lot, I know you disagree with at least one thing I said. What scotch do <em>you</em> like?</p> <p>&nbsp;</p> <p>&nbsp;</p> <div class="sharedaddy sd-sharing-enabled"><div class="robots-nocontent sd-block sd-social sd-social-icon sd-sharing"><h3
