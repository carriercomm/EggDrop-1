id="Order">Order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazil_nut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>The Brazil nut family is in the order <a href="/wiki/Ericales" title="Ericales">Ericales</a>, as are other well-known plants such as blueberries, cranberries, <a href="/wiki/Sapote" title="Sapote">sapote</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gutta-percha" title="Gutta-percha">gutta-percha</a>, tea, gooseberries, <a href="/wiki/Phlox" title="Phlox">phlox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Persimmon" title="Persimmon">persimmons</a>.</p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil_nut_tree">Brazil nut tree</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazil_nut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Brazil nut tree">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>The Brazil nut tree is the only species in the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic" title="Monotypic" class="mw-redirect">monotypic</a> genus <i>Bertholletia</i>. It is native to the <a href="/wiki/Guianas" title="Guianas" class="mw-redirect">Guianas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, and eastern <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>. It occurs as scattered trees in large forests on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rio_Negro_(Amazon)" title="Rio Negro (Amazon)">Rio Negro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tapaj%C3%B3s" title="Tapajós">Tapajós</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Orinoco" title="Orinoco">Orinoco</a>. The genus is named after the French chemist <a href="/wiki/Claude_Louis_Berthollet" title="Claude Louis Berthollet">Claude Louis Berthollet</a>.</p> <p>The Brazil nut is a large tree, reaching 50&#160;m (160&#160;ft) tall and with a trunk 1 to 2&#160;m (3.3 to 6.6&#160;ft) in diameter, making it among the largest of trees in the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazon rainforests</a>. It may live for 500 years or more, and according to some authorities often reaches an age of 1,000 years.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The stem is straight and commonly without branches for well over half the tree's height, with a large emergent crown of long branches above the surrounding canopy of other trees.</p> <p>The bark is grayish and smooth. The leaves are dry-season <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a>, alternate, simple, entire or crenate, oblong, 20–35&#160;cm (7.9–13.8&#160;in) long and 10–15&#160;cm (3.9–5.9&#160;in) broad. The flowers are small, greenish-white, in <a href="/wiki/Panicles" title="Panicles" class="mw-redirect">panicles</a> 5–10&#160;cm (2.0–3.9&#160;in) long; each flower has a two-parted, deciduous <a href="/wiki/Calyx_(botany)" title="Calyx (botany)" class="mw-redirect">calyx</a>, six unequal cream-colored petals, and numerous <a href="/wiki/Stamen" title="Stamen">stamens</a> united into a broad, hood-shaped mass.</p> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">Hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazil_nut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>In Brazil, it is illegal to cut down a Brazil nut tree. As a result, they can be found outside production areas, in the backyards of homes and near roads and streets. The fruit containing nuts is very heavy and rigid, and it poses a serious threat to vehicles and persons passing under the tree. At least one person has died after being hit on the head by a falling fruit.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> As the Brazil nut is a botanical seed, and unlike botanical nuts, the density of the fruit makes them sink in fresh water, which can cause clogging of waterways in riparian areas.</p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazil_nut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <div class="thumb tleft"> <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Brazil_nut_DSC05477.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Brazil_nut_DSC05477.JPG/220px-Brazil_nut_DSC05477.JPG" width="220" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Brazil_nut_DSC05477.JPG/330px-Brazil_nut_DSC05477.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Brazil_nut_DSC05477.JPG/440px-Brazil_nut_DSC05477.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1419" data-file-height="1292" /></a> <div class="thumbcaption"> <div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Brazil_nut_DSC05477.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div> A freshly cut Brazil nut fruit</div> </div> </div> <p>Brazil nut trees produce fruit almost exclusively in pristine forests, as disturbed forests lack the large-bodied bees of the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Bombus" title="Bombus" class="mw-redirect">Bombus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Centris" title="Centris">Centris</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Epicharis_(bee)" title="Epicharis (bee)">Epicharis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eulaema" title="Eulaema">Eulaema</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Xylocopa" title="Xylocopa" class="mw-redirect">Xylocopa</a></i> which are the only ones capable of <a href="/wiki/Pollinate" title="Pollinate" class="mw-redirect">pollinating</a> the tree's flowers, with different bee genera being the primary pollinators in different areas, and different times of year.<sup id="cite_ref-Nelson1985_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nelson1985-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Moritz1984_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moritz1984-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Brazil nuts have been harvested from plantations, but production is low and is currently not economically viable.<sup id="cite_ref-Industry_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Industry-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>The fruit takes 14 months to mature after pollination of the flowers. The fruit itself is a large capsule 10–15&#160;cm (3.9–5.9&#160;in) in diameter, resembling a coconut <a href="/wiki/Endocarp" title="Endocarp" class="mw-redirect">endocarp</a> in size and weighing up to 2&#160;kg (4.4&#160;lb). It has a hard, woody shell 8–12&#160;mm (0.31–0.47&#160;in) thick, which contains eight to 24 triangular seeds 4–5&#160;cm (1.6–2.0&#160;in) long (the "Brazil nuts") packed like the segments of an orange.</p> <p>The capsule contains a small hole at one end, which enables large rodents like the <a href="/wiki/Common_agouti" title="Common agouti">agouti</a> to gnaw it open. They then eat some of the nuts inside while burying others for later use; some of these are able to germinate into new Brazil nut trees. Most of the seeds are "planted" by the agoutis in shady places, and the young saplings may have to wait years, in a state of dormancy, for a tree to fall and sunlight to reach it, when it starts growing again. <a href="/wiki/Capuchin_monkey" title="Capuchin monkey">Capuchin monkeys</a> have been reported to open Brazil nuts using a stone as an <a href="/wiki/Anvil" title="Anvil">anvil</a>.</p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a American">Scientific American</a> Supplement</i>, No. 598, June 18, 1887</div> </div> </div> <p>Despite their name, the most significant exporter of Brazil nuts is not Brazil but Bolivia, where they are called <i>nuez de Brasil</i>. In Brazil, these nuts are called <i>castanhas-do-pará</i> (literally "chestnuts from <a href="/wiki/Par%C3%A1" title="Pará">Pará</a>"), but <a href="/wiki/Acre_(state)" title="Acre (state)">Acreans</a> call them <i>castanhas-do-acre</i> instead. <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Indigenous</a> names include <i>juvia</i> in the Orinoco area.</p> <p>Though it is commonly called the Brazil <b>nut</b>, in botanical terms it is the <b>seed</b> from the fruit of this tree. To a botanist, a nut is a hard-shelled <a href="/wiki/Dehiscence_(botany)" title="Dehiscence (botany)">indehiscent</a> <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a>.</p> <p>In the United States Brazil nuts were once known by the epithet "<a href="/wiki/Nigger" title="Nigger">nigger</a> toes,"<sup id="cite_ref-Matt_Brazil_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Matt_Brazil-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> though the term fell out of favor as public use of the racial slur became increasingly unacceptable. They can be seen being sold in a market under this name in a scene from the 1922 <a href="/wiki/Stan_Laurel" title="Stan Laurel">Stan Laurel</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pest_(1922_film)" title="The Pest (1922 film)">The Pest</a></i>.</p> <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nut_production">Nut production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazil_nut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nut production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> <p>Around 20,000 tons of Brazil nuts are harvested each year, of which Bolivia accounts for about 50%, Brazil 40%, and Peru 10% (2000 estimates).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1980, annual production was around 40,000 tons per year from Brazil alone, and in 1970, Brazil harvested a reported 104,487 tons of nuts.<sup id="cite_ref-Industry_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Industry-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_harvesting">Effects of harvesting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazil_nut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Effects of harvesting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>Brazil nuts for international trade can come from wild collection rather than from plantations. This has been advanced as a model for generating income from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_and_subtropical_moist_broadleaf_forests" title="Tropical and subtropical moist broadleaf forests">tropical forest</a> without destroying it. The nuts are gathered by migrant workers known as <i>castanheiros</i>.</p> <p>Analysis of tree ages in areas that are harvested show that moderate and intense gathering takes so many seeds, not enough are left to replace older trees as they die. Sites with light gathering activities had many young trees, while sites with intense gathering practices had hardly any young trees.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>Statistical tests were done to determine what environmental factors could be contributing to the lack of younger trees. The most consistent effect was found to be the level of gathering activity at a particular site. A <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">computer model</a> predicting the size of trees where people picked all the nuts matched the tree size data gathered from physical sites that had heavy harvesting.</p> <p>Upon harvesting and collecting the ripened cases that fall off the trees harvesters have to be cautious because they are easily heavy enough to kill a person. Fatal accidents are not uncommon among collectors – they stop work at once if the wind suddenly strengthens, because this can cause a bombardment.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a calories come from fat. The fat breakdown is roughly 25% <a href="/wiki/Saturated_fat" title="Saturated fat">saturated</a>, 41% <a href="/wiki/Monounsaturated_fat" title="Monounsaturated fat">monounsaturated</a>, and 34% <a href="/wiki/Polyunsaturated_fat" title="Polyunsaturated fat">polyunsaturated</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Due to their high polyunsaturated fat content, primarily <a href="/wiki/Omega-6_fatty_acid" title="Omega-6 fatty acid">omega-6 fatty acids</a>, shelled Brazil nuts soon become <a href="/wiki/Rancidification" title="Rancidification">rancid</a>.</p> <p>Nutritionally, Brazil nuts are a good source of some vitamins and minerals. A cup (133&#160;grams) of Brazil nuts contains the vitamins thiamin (0.8&#160;mg—55% DV) and vitamin E (7.6&#160;mg—38% DV); minerals calcium (213&#160;mg—21% DV), magnesium (500&#160;mg—125% DV), phosphorus (946&#160;mg—96% DV), copper (2.3&#160;mg—116% DV), and manganese (1.6&#160;mg—81%).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Brazil nuts are perhaps the richest dietary source of <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a>; 28&#160;g (1&#160;oz, 6–8 nuts) can contain as much as 544&#160;µg.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> This is 10 times the adult U.S. <a href="/wiki/Reference_Daily_Intake" title="Reference Daily Intake">Recommended Dietary Allowances</a>, more even than the <a href="/wiki/Tolerable_upper_intake_level#Current_recommendations" title="Tolerable upper intake level" class="mw-redirect">Tolerable Upper Intake Level</a>, although the amount of selenium within batches of nuts varies greatly.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Brazil nuts and their oil are mainly used as a food in the United States. Brazil nut oil is clear yellowish oil with a pleasant, sweet smell and taste. It makes a wonderful light oil for salad dressing.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p><br /> Despite the possible health benefits of the nut, the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> has imposed strict regulations on the import from Brazil of Brazil nuts in their shells, as the shells have been found to contain high levels of <a href="/wiki/Aflatoxin" title="Aflatoxin">aflatoxins</a>, which can lead to liver cancer.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>Brazil nuts contain small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Radium" title="Radium">radium</a>. Although the amount of radium, a <a href="/wiki/Radioactivity" title="Radioactivity" class="mw-redirect">radioactive</a> element, is very small, about 1–7 nCi/g<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> (40–260 <a href="/wiki/Becquerel" title="Becquerel">Bq</a>/kg), and most of it is not retained by the body, this is 1000 times higher than in other foods. According to <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_Associated_Universities" title="Oak Ridge Associated Universities">Oak Ridge Associated Universities</a>, this is not because of elevated levels of radium in the soil, but due to "the very extensive root system of the tree."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>In the United Kingdom, Brazil nuts are the second most common cause of nut allergic reactions. There has been one known, published and confirmed case of an allergic reaction to Brazil nuts resulting from sexual transmission.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p> <p>Brazil nuts are a common ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Mixed_nuts" title="Mixed nuts">mixed nuts</a> where, because of their large size, they tend to rise to the top, an example of <a href="/wiki/Granular_convection" title="Granular convection">granular convection</a>, which for this reason is often called the "Brazil nut effect".</p> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span oil is often used in soaps, shampoos, and hair conditioning/repair products in South America, and this use is beginning to catch on in the United States as well.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. Engravings in Brazil nut shells were supposedly used as decorative jewelery by the indigenous tribes in Bolivia, although no examples still exist. Because of its hardness, Brazil nut shell has often been pulverized and used as an abrasive to polish softer materials such as metals and even ceramics (in the same way as <a href="/wiki/Jeweler%27s_rouge" title="Jeweler's rouge" class="mw-redirect">jeweler's rouge</a> is used). A high luster could be acquired by a final application of <a href="/wiki/Carnauba_wax" title="Carnauba wax">carnauba wax</a>, only produced in north-eastern Brazil.</p> <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wood">Wood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brazil_nut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Wood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> <p>The lumber from Brazil nut trees (not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Brazilwood" title="Brazilwood" class="mw-redirect">Brazilwood</a>) is of excellent quality, but logging the trees is prohibited by law in all three producing countries (Brazil, Bolivia and Peru).
